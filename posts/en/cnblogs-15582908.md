# Emotional Chatting Machine: Emotional Conversation Generation with Internal and External Memory

## Abstract

Emotion perception and expression are key factors for the success of dialogue systems or conversational agents. However, this issue had not yet been studied in large-scale dialogue generation. In this paper, the authors propose Emotional Chatting Machine, or ECM, which can generate responses that are appropriate not only in content, meaning relevance and grammaticality, but also in emotion, meaning emotional consistency. To the best of the authors' knowledge, this is the first work to address emotional factors in large-scale dialogue generation. ECM uses three new mechanisms:

- Modeling high-level abstractions of emotional expression through emotion-category embeddings.

- Capturing changes in implicit emotional state.

- Using explicit emotional expressions through an external emotion vocabulary.

Experiments show that the model can generate responses that are appropriate both in content and in emotion.

## Introduction

As an important part of human intelligence, emotional intelligence is defined as the ability to perceive, integrate, understand, and regulate emotion (Mayer and Salovey, 1997). Enabling machines to understand affect and emotion has long been a goal of artificial intelligence (Picard and Picard, 1997). To build a chatbot that can communicate with users at a human level, machines need the ability to perceive and express emotion.

Existing studies show that handling affect and emotion in dialogue systems or conversational agents can improve user satisfaction (Prendinger, Mori, and Ishizuka, 2005) and reduce failures in dialogue (Martinovski and Traum, 2003). Some early work also adjusted dialogue behavior according to the user's emotional state (Polzin and Waibel, 2000), or generated responses to user utterances at both content-related and affect-related levels (Skowron, 2010).

Most of these studies, however, are inspired by psychology, rule-based, or limited to small-scale data. Recently, neural models trained on large-scale data have significantly improved open-domain dialogue generation (Ritter, Cherry, and Dolan, 2011; Vinyals and Le, 2015; Shang, Lu, and Li, 2015; Serban et al., 2016). These models mainly aim to improve content quality in dialogue generation (Gu et al., 2016; Li et al., 2016a; Xing et al., 2017; Mou et al., 2016; Li et al., 2016b). To the best of the authors' knowledge, emotional factors had not been addressed in existing neural models for conversational generation. Table 1 shows examples with and without emotion. ECM is more emotionally engaging and empathetic.

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15582908/image-01.png)

There are several challenges in handling emotional factors for large-scale conversation generation.

First, large-scale corpora rarely contain high-quality emotion-labeled data, because emotion annotation is subjective and emotion classification is challenging.

Second, emotion must be considered in a natural and coherent way, because grammaticality and emotional expression need to be balanced, as discussed by Ghosh et al. (2017).

Finally, as the experiments show, simply embedding emotion information into existing neural models cannot generate satisfactory emotional responses. It tends to produce generic expressions that are hard to perceive emotionally. These expressions contain only common words whose emotional signals are implicit or ambiguous, and they account for 73.7% of all emotional responses in the dataset.

This paper studies the problem of generating emotional responses in open-domain dialogue systems and proposes Emotional Chatting Machine, abbreviated as ECM. To obtain large-scale emotion-labeled data for ECM, the authors train a neural classifier on a manually annotated corpus. The classifier is then used to automatically annotate large-scale conversation data for ECM training. To express emotion naturally and coherently in sentences, the authors design a Seq2Seq generation model with new mechanisms for emotional-expression generation: emotion-category embeddings to capture high-level abstractions of emotional expression, an internal emotion state to dynamically balance grammaticality and emotionality, and external emotion memory to help generate more readable and explicit emotional expressions.

In summary, the paper makes the following contributions:

- It proposes handling emotional factors in large-scale conversation generation. To the authors' knowledge, this is the first research work on this topic.

- It proposes an end-to-end framework, ECM, that integrates emotional influence into large-scale dialogue generation. It includes three new mechanisms: emotion-category embedding, internal emotion memory, and external memory.

- Results show that compared with the traditional Seq2Seq model, ECM can generate responses with higher content and emotion scores. Future work such as empathetic computer agents and affective interaction models can be built on top of ECM.

## Related Work

In human-computer interaction, the ability to detect signs of human emotion and respond appropriately can enrich communication. For example, empathetic emotional expression strengthens user performance (Partala and Surakka 2004) and improves user satisfaction (Prendinger, Mori, and Ishizuka 2005). Experiments by Prendinger and Ishizuka (2005) show that empathetic computer agents can promote a more positive perception of interaction. Martinovski and Traum (2003) point out that many errors can be avoided if machines can recognize the user's emotional state and respond sensitively. Polzin and Waibel (2000) introduce how to adapt dialogue behavior according to the user's emotional state. Skowron (2010) proposes a dialogue system called affect listeners, which can respond to users' utterances at both content-related and affect-related levels.

These studies are mostly inspired by findings from psychology, rule-based, or limited to small-scale data, so they are difficult to apply to large-scale dialogue generation. Recently, Seq2Seq generation models (Sutskever, Vinyals, and Le 2014; Bahdanau, Cho, and Bengio 2014) have been successfully applied to large-scale dialogue generation (Vinyals and Le 2015), including neural responding machines (Shang, Lu, and Li 2015), hierarchical recurrent models (Serban et al. 2015), and many other models. These models focus on improving content quality, including increasing diversity (Li et al. 2016a), considering extra information (Xing et al. 2017; Mou et al. 2016; Li et al. 2016b; Herzig et al. 2017), and handling unknown words (Gu et al. 2016).

However, none of these studies addresses emotional factors in large-scale conversation generation. There are also several studies on generating text with controllable variables. Hu et al. (2017) propose a generation model that can produce sentences according to certain linguistic attributes such as sentiment and tense. Ghosh et al. (2017) propose an affective language model that generates text based on contextual words and emotion categories. Cagan, Frank, and Tsarfaty (2017) incorporate syntactic information and use sentiment viewpoints and topics to generate document comments. ECM differs from these studies in two major ways:

- Previous work depends heavily on linguistic tools or custom parameters, while ECM is fully data-driven and does not require manual adjustment.

- Previous work cannot model diverse emotional interactions between input and response. The generated text merely continues the dominant emotion in the context.

## Emotional Chatting Machine

### Background: Encoder-Decoder Framework

The model is based on the encoder-decoder framework in the general sequence-to-sequence, or Seq2Seq, model (Sutskever, Vinyals, and Le 2014). It is implemented with gated recurrent units (GRUs) (Cho et al. 2014; Chung et al. 2014). The encoder converts an input sequence \(\boldsymbol{X}=\left(x_{1}, x_{2}, \cdots, x_{n}\right)\) into hidden representations \(\boldsymbol{h}=\left(h_{1}, h_{2}, \cdots, h_{n}\right)\):

\[\boldsymbol{h}_{t}=\mathbf{G} \mathbf{R U}\left(\boldsymbol{h}_{t-1}, x_{t}\right)
\]

The decoder takes the context vector \(c_{t}\) and the embedding of the previously decoded word \(\boldsymbol{e}\left(y_{t-1}\right)\) as input, and uses another GRU to update its state \(\boldsymbol{s}_{t}\):

\[\boldsymbol{s}_{t}=\mathbf{G} \mathbf{R} \mathbf{U}\left(\boldsymbol{s}_{t-1},\left[\boldsymbol{c}_{t} ; \boldsymbol{e}\left(y_{t-1}\right)\right]\right)
\]

Here, \(\left[\boldsymbol{c}_{t} ; \boldsymbol{e}\left(y_{t-1}\right)\right]\) is the concatenation of two vectors used as the input to the GRU cell. The context vector \(c_{t}\) is designed to dynamically attend to important input information during decoding (Bahdanau, Cho, and Bengio 2014). After the state vector \(\boldsymbol{s}_{t}\) is obtained, the decoder generates a token by sampling from the output probability distribution \(\boldsymbol{o}_{t}\) computed from \(\boldsymbol{s}_{t}\):

\[\begin{aligned}
y_{t} \sim \boldsymbol{o}_{t} &=P\left(y_{t} \mid y_{1}, y_{2}, \cdots, y_{t-1}, \boldsymbol{c}_{t}\right) \\
&=\operatorname{softmax}\left(\mathbf{W}_{\mathbf{o}} \boldsymbol{s}_{t}\right)
\end{aligned}
\]

### Task Definition and Overview

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15582908/image-02.png)

The problem is defined as follows.

Given an input \(\boldsymbol{X}=\left(x_{1}, x_{2}, \cdots, x_{n}\right)\) and an emotion category \(e\) for the response to be generated, the goal is to generate a response \(\boldsymbol{Y}=\left(y_{1}, y_{2}, \cdots, y_{m}\right)\) consistent with emotion category \(e\). Fundamentally, the model estimates:

\[P(\boldsymbol{Y} \mid \boldsymbol{X}, e)=\prod_{t=1}^{m} P\left(y_{t} \mid y_{<t}, \boldsymbol{X}, e\right)
\]

The emotion categories are {Angry, Disgust, Happy, Like, Sad, Other}, following the Chinese emotion classification challenge task.

The paper assumes that the emotion category of the response to be generated is given, because emotion is highly subjective. Given one input, multiple emotion categories may be appropriate for the response, depending on the respondent's attitude. For example, for a sad story, one person may respond with sympathy as a friend, another may respond with anger as an irritable stranger, and another may respond happily as an enemy. This flexible emotional interaction between input and response is an important difference from previous studies (Hu et al. 2017; Ghosh et al. 2017; Cagan, Frank, and Tsarfaty 2017), where responses use the same sentiment or emotion as the input.

Therefore, because emotional response is subjective, the paper focuses on the core problem: generating an emotional response given an input and the desired response emotion category. The model works without deciding the response emotion category itself. There are multiple ways for a chatbot to choose an emotion category for response. One is to give the chatbot a personality and background knowledge. Another is to use training data to find the most common response emotion for the emotion in a given input and use that as the response emotion. This is reasonable because it reflects common human emotional tendencies. The authors leave this to future work.

Based on the generation framework discussed above, ECM uses three mechanisms to generate emotional expression:

- Since emotion categories are high-level abstractions of emotional expression, ECM embeds emotion categories and feeds the category embedding back into the decoder.

- The authors assume that an internal emotion state exists during decoding. To capture implicit changes in this state and dynamically balance grammar and emotion, ECM uses an internal memory module.

- Explicit emotional expression is modeled by selecting either general, non-emotional words or emotional words through an external memory module.

Figure 1 gives an overview of ECM. During training, a corpus of post-response pairs is fed into an emotion classifier to generate emotion labels for each response. ECM is then trained on triples: post, response, and response emotion label. During inference, a post is fed into ECM to generate emotional responses under different emotion categories.

### Emotion Category Embedding

Because emotion categories such as Angry, Disgust, and Happy provide high-level abstractions of emotional expression, the most intuitive way to model emotion in response generation is to use the emotion category of the target response as an additional input. Each emotion category is represented by a low-dimensional real-valued vector. For each emotion category \(e\), the vector \(\boldsymbol{v}_{e}\) is randomly initialized and then learned during training. The emotion-category embedding \(\boldsymbol{v}_{e}\), word embedding \(\boldsymbol{e}(y_{t-1})\), and context vector \(\boldsymbol{c}_ {t}\) are fed into the decoder to update decoder state \(\boldsymbol{s}_{\boldsymbol{t}}\):

\[\boldsymbol{s}_{\boldsymbol{t}}=\mathbf{G} \mathbf{R} \mathbf{U}\left(\boldsymbol{s}_{t-1},\left[\boldsymbol{c}_{t} ; \boldsymbol{e}\left(y_{t-1}\right) ; \boldsymbol{v}_{e}\right]\right)
\]

Based on \(\boldsymbol{s}_{\boldsymbol{t}}\), the decoding probability distribution can be computed as above to generate the next token \(y_{t}\).

### Internal Memory

The method described in the previous section is relatively static: the emotion-category embedding does not change during generation, which may damage grammatical correctness, as discussed by Ghosh et al. (2017). Inspired by psychological findings that emotional responses are relatively brief and involve change (Gross 1998; Hochschild 1979), and by dynamic emotional context in affective responses (Alam, Danieli, and Riccardi 2017), the authors design an internal memory module to capture emotional dynamics during decoding. The emotional-expression process is modeled as follows: before decoding begins, each category has an internal emotion state; at each step, the emotion state decays to some extent; once decoding is complete, the emotion state should decay to zero, indicating that the emotion has been fully expressed.

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15582908/image-03.png)

The details of the internal memory module are shown in Figure 2. At each step \(t\), ECM uses the previous word embedding \(\boldsymbol{e}(y_{t}-1)\), the decoder's previous state \(\boldsymbol{s}_{t-1}\), and the current context vector \(\boldsymbol{c}_{t}\) to compute the read gate. The write gate is computed on the decoder state vector \(s_{t}\). The read and write gates are defined as:

\[\begin{aligned}
\boldsymbol{g}_{t}^{r} &=\operatorname{sigmoid}\left(\mathbf{W}_{\mathbf{g}}^{\mathbf{r}}\left[\boldsymbol{e}\left(y_{t-1}\right) ; \boldsymbol{s}_{t-1} ; \boldsymbol{c}_{t}\right]\right) \\
\boldsymbol{g}_{t}^{w} &=\operatorname{sigmoid}\left(\mathbf{W}_{\mathbf{g}}^{\mathbf{w}} \boldsymbol{s}_{t}\right)
\end{aligned}
\]

The read gate and write gate are used to read from and write to the internal memory module. Therefore, at each step, a certain amount \(\boldsymbol{g}_{t}^{w}\) of emotion state is erased. At the final step, the internal emotion state decays to zero. The process is described as:

\[\begin{aligned}
\boldsymbol{M}_{r, t}^{I} &=\boldsymbol{g}_{t}^{r} \otimes \boldsymbol{M}_{e, t}^{I} \\
\boldsymbol{M}_{e, t+1}^{I} &=\boldsymbol{g}_{t}^{w} \otimes \boldsymbol{M}_{e, t}^{I}
\end{aligned}
\]

Here, \(\otimes\) means element-wise multiplication, \(r/w\) mean read/write, and \(I\) means internal. The GRU updates its state \(\boldsymbol{s}_{t}\) using the previous target word \(\boldsymbol{e}(y_{t}-1)\), the previous decoder state \(\boldsymbol{s}_{t-1}\), the context vector \(\boldsymbol{c}_{t}\), and the emotion-state update \(\boldsymbol{M}_{r, t}^{I}\):

\[\boldsymbol{s}_{\boldsymbol{t}}=\mathbf{G} \mathbf{R} \mathbf{U}\left(\boldsymbol{s}_{t-1},\left[\boldsymbol{c}_{t} ; \boldsymbol{e}\left(y_{t-1}\right) ; \boldsymbol{M}_{r, t}^{I}\right]\right)
\]

Based on this state, the word-generation distribution \(\boldsymbol{o}_{t}\) can be obtained and the next word \(y_{t}\) can be sampled. After generating the next word, \(\boldsymbol{M}_{r, t+1}^{I}\) is written back to internal memory. Repeated application of the write equation is equivalent to continuous matrix multiplication and produces a decay effect. This is similar to the erase operation in memory networks (Miller et al. 2016).

### External Memory

In the internal memory module, the relationship between changes in internal emotion state and word selection is implicit and cannot be directly observed. Since emotional expression differs strongly between emotional words such as `lovely` and `awesome`, and general non-emotional words such as `person` and `day`, the authors propose an external memory module that explicitly models emotional expression by assigning different generation probabilities to emotional words and general words. This allows the model to choose whether to generate from the emotion vocabulary or the general vocabulary.

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15582908/image-04.png)

The decoder with external memory is shown in Figure 3. Given the current decoder state \(s_{t}\), an emotion softmax \(P_{e}(y_{t}＝w_{e})\) is computed for the emotion vocabulary read from external memory, and a general softmax \(P_{g}(y_{t}＝w_{g})\) is computed for the general vocabulary. A type selector \(α_{t}\) controls the weight for generating an emotional word or a general word \(y_{t}\). Finally, the next word is sampled from the next-word probability distribution, which is the concatenation of the two weighted probabilities:

\[\begin{aligned}
\alpha_{t} &=\operatorname{sigmoid}\left(\mathbf{v}_{\mathbf{u}}{ }^{\top} s_{t}\right), \\
P_{g}\left(y_{t}=w_{g}\right) &=\operatorname{softmax}\left(\mathbf{W}_{\mathbf{g}}^{\mathrm{o}} s_{t}\right), \\
P_{e}\left(y_{t}=w_{e}\right) &=\operatorname{softmax}\left(\mathbf{W}_{\mathrm{e}}^{\mathrm{o}} s_{t}\right), \\
y_{t} \sim \boldsymbol{o}_{t}=P\left(y_{t}\right) &=\left[\begin{array}{r}
\left(1-\alpha_{t}\right) P_{g}\left(y_{t}=w_{g}\right) \\
\alpha_{t} P_{e}\left(y_{t}=w_{e}\right)
\end{array}\right],
\end{aligned}
\]

Here, \(\alpha_{t}\in[0,1]\) is a scalar that balances the choice between emotional word \(w_{e}\) and general word \(w_{g}\). \(P_{g}/P_{e}\) are the distributions over general words and emotional words respectively, and \(P_{(y_{t})}\) is the final word-decoding distribution. The two vocabularies do not overlap, and the final distribution is their concatenation.

### Loss Function

The loss function is the cross-entropy error between the predicted token distribution \(\boldsymbol{o}_{t}\) and the gold distribution \(\boldsymbol{p}_{t}\) in the training corpus. In addition, two regularization terms are applied: one on internal memory, forcing the internal emotion state to decay to zero at the end of decoding, and one on external memory, constraining the selection between emotional and general words. For one sample:

\[L(\theta)=-\sum_{t=1}^{m} \boldsymbol{p}_{t} \log \left(\boldsymbol{o}_{t}\right)-\sum_{t=1}^{m} q_{t} \log \left(\alpha_{t}\right)+\left\|\boldsymbol{M}_{e, m}^{I}\right\|
\]

where \(\boldsymbol{M}_{e, m}^{I}\) is the internal emotion state at the final step \(m\), \(α_{t}\) is the probability of selecting an emotional word or a general word, and \(q_{t}∈ \{0,1\}\) is the correct choice for emotional or general words in \(\boldsymbol{Y}\). The second term supervises the probability of selecting emotional or general words. The third term ensures that once generation is complete, the internal emotion state has been fully expressed.

## Data Preparation

Because there is no ready-made data to train ECM, the authors first train an emotion classifier using the NLPCC emotion classification dataset, then use the classifier to annotate the STC dialogue dataset (Shang, Lu, and Li 2015), building their own experimental dataset. Data preparation has two steps.

**1. Build an emotion classifier**

Several classifiers are trained on the NLPCC dataset, and the best one is selected for automatic annotation. The dataset was used for emotion classification challenge tasks in NLPCC2013 and NLPCC2014 and contains 23,105 sentences collected from Weibo. It is manually annotated with eight emotion categories: Angry, Disgust, Fear, Happy, Like, Sad, Surprise, and Other. After removing uncommon categories, Fear at 1.5% and Surprise at 4.4%, there are six categories: Angry, Disgust, Happy, Like, Sad, and Other.

The NLPCC dataset is split into training, validation, and test sets at an 8:1:1 ratio. Several emotion classifiers are trained on the filtered dataset, including a lexicon-based classifier (Liu 2012), using the emotion lexicon from Xu et al. (2008), RNN (Mikolov et al. 2010), LSTM (Hochreiter and Schmidhuber 1997), and bidirectional LSTM (Bi-LSTM) (Graves, Fernandez, and Schmidhuber 2005). The results in Table 2 show that all neural-network classifiers outperform the lexicon-based classifier, and the best-performing one is Bi-LSTM with accuracy 0.623.

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15582908/image-05.png)

**2. Annotate STC with emotion**

The best classifier, Bi-LSTM, is used to annotate the STC dataset with six emotion categories. After annotation, the authors obtain an emotion-labeled dataset called Emotional STC, or ESTC. The statistics of ESTC are shown in Table 3. Although the emotion labels in ESTC are noisy because of automatic annotation, the dataset is sufficient for training the model in practice. As future work, the authors plan to study how classification errors affect response generation.

## Experiments

### Implementation Details

TensorFlow is used to implement the proposed model. The encoder and decoder are both two-layer GRU structures, each layer with 256 hidden units, and they use different parameter sets. Word embedding size is set to 100. Vocabulary size is limited to 40,000. Emotion-category embedding size is set to 100. Internal memory is a trainable matrix of size 6 x 256, and external memory is a list of 40,000 words containing general and emotional words, with emotional words marked differently. To generate diverse responses, beam search is used during decoding with beam size 20. After removing responses containing unknown words, responses are reranked by generation probability.

Mini-batch stochastic gradient descent (SGD) is used. Batch size and learning rate are set to 128 and 0.5 respectively. To speed up training, a Seq2Seq model with pretrained word embeddings is first trained on the STC dataset. Then ECM is trained on the ESTC dataset, with parameters initialized from the pretrained Seq2Seq model. The authors run 20 epochs, and training each model takes about one week on a Titan X GPU machine.

### Baseline Models

As described above, this is the first paper to explore emotional factors in large-scale conversation generation. The authors did not find closely related baselines in the literature. Affect-LM (Ghosh et al. 2017) cannot be used as a baseline because it cannot generate different-emotion responses to the same post. It simply copies and uses the emotion of the input post. It also heavily relies on language resources and requires manual parameter adjustment.

The paper selects two appropriate baselines: a general Seq2Seq model (Sutskever, Vinyals, and Le 2014) and an emotion-category embedding model created by the authors, called Emb. In Emb, the emotion category is embedded into a vector and used as input at every decoding position, similar to the idea of user embeddings (Li et al. 2016b). Since emotion categories are high-level abstractions of emotional expression, this is an appropriate baseline.

### Automatic Evaluation

**Metrics**

As noted by Liu et al. (2016), BLEU is not suitable for measuring dialogue generation because it has low correlation with human judgment. At the content level, meaning relevance and grammaticality, the paper uses perplexity to evaluate the model. At the emotion level, it uses emotion accuracy to measure consistency between the expected emotion category, given as model input, and the predicted emotion category of the generated response from an emotion classifier.

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15582908/image-07.png)

**Results**

The results are shown in Table 4. ECM performs best in emotion accuracy, and its perplexity is better than Seq2Seq but worse than Emb. This may be because ECM's loss function is supervised not only on perplexity but also on the choice between general and emotional words, as shown in Equation 15. In practice, emotion accuracy is more important than perplexity because the generated sentences are already fluent and grammatical at perplexity 68.0.

To study the effect of different modules, the authors conduct ablation tests, removing one of ECM's three modules at a time. ECM without external memory obtains the best perplexity. By introducing internal memory, the model can generate responses without sacrificing grammaticality, because the module dynamically balances grammar and emotion. Removing external memory causes the largest decrease in emotion accuracy, showing that external memory improves emotional accuracy by explicitly selecting emotion words. Seq2Seq has extremely low emotion accuracy because it generates the same response for different emotion categories.

### Human Evaluation

To better analyze generated-response quality from both content and emotion perspectives, the authors conduct manual evaluation. Given a post and an emotion category, responses generated by all models are randomly assigned to three human annotators.

**Metrics**

Annotators are asked to score responses for content, in the range 0, 1, 2, and emotion, in the range 0, 1, and to state which of two systems they prefer. Content measures whether the response to the post is appropriate, natural, and likely to be produced by a human. This is a commonly used metric in research and dialogue challenges, from Shang, Lu, and Li (2015). Emotion measures whether the emotional expression of the response is consistent with the given emotion category.

**Annotation Statistics**

The authors randomly sample 200 posts from the test set. For each model, 1,200 responses are generated in total. For Seq2Seq, the top 6 responses are generated for each post. For Emb and ECM, the top 6 responses corresponding to six emotion categories are generated.

Fleiss' kappa (Fleiss 1971) is used to measure inter-annotator agreement. Fleiss' kappa for content and emotion is 0.441 and 0.757 respectively, indicating moderate agreement and substantial agreement.

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15582908/image-08.png)

**Results**

The results are shown in Table 6. ECM's response scores are significantly better than other methods on both metrics, according to a two-tailed t-test, with \(p < 0.05\) for content and \(p < 0.005\) for emotion. Compared with Emb, after adding internal memory and external memory, ECM improves emotion-level performance, showing that it can generate more explicit emotional expressions. Content performance also improves from 1.256 in Emb to 1.299 in ECM, showing that ECM can control emotional weight and generate content-appropriate responses.

For all emotion categories, ECM outperforms other methods at the emotion level. However, ECM's content-level performance for Disgust and Angry is worse than the baseline models because these two categories lack enough training data. For example, in ESTC, the angry category has only 234,635 responses, far fewer than other categories.

To evaluate whether ECM can generate responses appropriate in both content and emotion, the authors consider content and emotion scores together, as shown in Table 5. Among ECM-generated responses, 27.2% have content score 2 and emotion score 1, while Emb and Seq2Seq have only 22.8% and 9.0% respectively. This shows that ECM generates high-quality responses better in both content and emotion.

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15582908/image-09.png)

**Preference Test**

In addition, emotional models, Emb and ECM, are preferred over Seq2Seq. Compared with other methods, ECM is favored by annotators, as shown in Table 7, with a two-tailed t-test of \(p < 0.001\). Responses with different emotions are more engaging than the generic responses produced by Seq2Seq. ECM is preferred because of explicit emotional expression and content appropriateness.

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15582908/image-10.png)

### Emotional Interaction Analysis and Case Study

Figure 5 shows emotional interaction patterns between posts and responses in ESTC. An emotional interaction pattern, or EIP, is a pair of emotion categories for a post and its response. The value of an EIP is the conditional probability \(P(e_{r} | e_{p})=P(e_{r},e_{p})/P(e_{p})\). Darker EIPs occur more frequently than lighter ones. Several observations can be made. First, frequent EIPs show that for each post emotion category, there are several dominant response emotions. For example, when a post expresses Happy, the response is usually Like or Happy. Second, the diagonal pattern indicates empathy, a common type of emotional interaction. Third, there are other EIPs for posts, showing that emotional interaction in dialogue is diverse. The Other emotion category contains far more data than other categories, as shown in Table 3, so EIPs are biased toward this category because of data bias and emotion-classification errors.

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15582908/image-11.png)

Some examples are shown in Figure 4. For a given post, multiple emotion categories can be appropriate for a response in a dialogue. Seq2Seq generates responses with random emotions. ECM can generate an emotional response according to each emotion category. All these responses can be appropriate for the post, which explains why multiple EIPs exist and why the emotion category should be specified as input to the system.

If the predefined emotion category and the post emotion belong to one of the common EIPs, ECM can generate an appropriate response. Colored words show that ECM can explicitly express emotion through the external memory module, which can choose general non-emotional words or emotional words during decoding. For low-frequency EIPs, such as <Happy, Disgust> and <Happy, Angry> in the last two rows of Figure 4, responses may not fit the emotion category because of insufficient training data and/or errors from emotion classification.

## Conclusion and Future Work

This paper proposes Emotional Chatting Machine, or ECM, to model emotional influence in large-scale dialogue generation. It proposes three mechanisms for emotional factors: emotion-category embedding, internal emotion memory, and external memory. Objective and manual evaluations show that ECM can generate appropriate responses at both the content level and the emotion level.

In future work, the authors plan to explore interaction between affect and ECM: instead of specifying the emotion category, the model should determine the most appropriate emotion category for the response. This may be challenging because the task depends on topic, context, and the user's emotion.
