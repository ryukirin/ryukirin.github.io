# Emotional Chatting Machine Paper Notes

## Background

Most previous research, before this paper, was inspired by psychology, either rule-based or limited to small-scale data. Models trained on large-scale data did not consider emotional factors.

This research has several challenges:

- Lack of databases with emotion labels.

- Need to balance grammatical expression and emotional expression.

- Existing models only embed emotions in a simple way, and usually generate emotionless utterances that are implicit and ambiguous.

This paper makes the following improvements:

- The paper proposes handling emotional factors in large-scale conversation generation. To the authors' knowledge, this is the first research work on this topic.

- The paper proposes an end-to-end framework, called ECM, that integrates emotional influence into large-scale dialogue generation. It contains three new mechanisms: emotion category embedding, internal emotion memory, and external memory.

- Results show that compared with the traditional Seq2Seq model, ECM can generate responses with higher content and emotion scores. The authors believe that future research, such as empathetic computer agents and emotional interaction models, can be built on ECM.

Compared with existing studies, this work differs in two main ways:

- Previous studies heavily rely on linguistic tools or customized parameters in text generation, while this model is fully data-driven and does not require manual tuning.

- Previous studies cannot model multiple emotional interactions between input and response. The generated text simply continues the emotion of the main context.

## Model

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15591030/image-01.png)

Given an input \(\boldsymbol{X}=\left(x_{1}, x_{2}, \cdots, x_{n}\right)\) and the emotional category \(e\) of the response to be generated, explained below, the goal is to generate a response \(\boldsymbol{Y}=\left(y_{1}, y_{2}, \cdots, y_{m}\right)\) consistent with emotional category \(e\). Fundamentally, the model estimates the following probability:

\[P(\boldsymbol{Y} \mid \boldsymbol{X}, e)=\prod_{t=1}^{m} P\left(y_{t} \mid y_{<t}, \boldsymbol{X}, e\right)
\]

The emotion categories are `{ Angry, Disgust, Happy, Like, Sad, Other }`, following the Chinese emotion classification challenge.

The proposed Emotional Chatting Machine, ECM, uses three mechanisms to generate emotional expression:

- Since emotion categories are high-level abstractions of emotional expression, ECM embeds emotion categories and feeds the emotion category embedding back to the decoder.

- The authors assume that during decoding, there is an internal emotional state. To capture implicit changes in this state and dynamically balance the weights between grammatical state and emotional state, ECM uses an internal memory module.

- Through an external memory module, ECM explicitly selects general, non-emotional words or emotional words, thereby modeling explicit emotional expression.

### Emotion category embedding

For each emotion category \(e\), the vector \(\boldsymbol{v}_e\) of the emotion category is randomly initialized, then learned through training. The emotion category embedding \(\boldsymbol{v}_e\), word embedding \(\boldsymbol{e}(y_{t-1})\), and context vector \(\boldsymbol{c}_t\) are fed into the decoder to update decoder state \(\boldsymbol{s}_t\):

\[\boldsymbol{s}_{\boldsymbol{t}}=\mathbf{G} \mathbf{R} \mathbf{U}\left(\boldsymbol{s}_{t-1},\left[\boldsymbol{c}_{t} ; \boldsymbol{e}\left(y_{t-1}\right) ; \boldsymbol{v}_{e}\right]\right)
\]

Based on \(\boldsymbol{s}_t\), the decoding probability distribution can be computed through the previous formula to generate the next token \(y_t\).

### Internal memory

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15591030/image-02.png)

The purpose of internal memory is to balance grammaticality and emotionality. The method used in the paper is that at every step, the emotional state decays to some extent. Once decoding is complete, the emotional state should decay to zero, indicating that the emotion has been fully expressed.

My understanding: if the emotional state stays unchanged all the time, the emotion weight may become too high and harm the grammar of the sentence. For example, in "I'm happy because of the weather", the emotion-state weight is high in the first half, so the model chooses "happy" to express emotion. As the emotion weight gradually decreases, the second half can choose a declarative clause rather than something like "because of the happy".

The detailed process of the internal memory module is shown in Figure 2. At each step `t`, ECM uses the word embedding of the previously decoded word \(\boldsymbol{e}(y_t-1)\), the previous decoder state \(\boldsymbol{s}_{t-1}\), and current context vector \(\boldsymbol{c}_t\) to compute the read gate \(\boldsymbol{g}_t^r\). The write gate \(\boldsymbol{g}^w\) is computed on the decoder state vector \(s_t\). The read and write gates are defined as follows, in Equations 6 and 7:

\[\begin{aligned}
\boldsymbol{g}_{t}^{r} &=\operatorname{sigmoid}\left(\mathbf{W}_{\mathbf{g}}^{\mathbf{r}}\left[\boldsymbol{e}\left(y_{t-1}\right) ; \boldsymbol{s}_{t-1} ; \boldsymbol{c}_{t}\right]\right) \\
\boldsymbol{g}_{t}^{w} &=\operatorname{sigmoid}\left(\mathbf{W}_{\mathbf{g}}^{\mathbf{w}} \boldsymbol{s}_{t}\right)
\end{aligned}
\]

The read gate and write gate are used to read from and write to the internal memory module, respectively. Therefore, the emotional state is erased by a certain amount, \(\boldsymbol{g}_t^w\), at each step. At the final step, the internal emotional state decays to zero. This process is formally described as Equations 8 and 9:

\[\begin{aligned}
\boldsymbol{M}_{r, t}^{I} &=\boldsymbol{g}_{t}^{r} \otimes \boldsymbol{M}_{e, t}^{I} \\
\boldsymbol{M}_{e, t+1}^{I} &=\boldsymbol{g}_{t}^{w} \otimes \boldsymbol{M}_{e, t}^{I}
\end{aligned}
\]

Here \(\otimes\) denotes [element-wise multiplication](https://www.zhihu.com/question/65866370), `r/w` represent read/write, and `I` means internal. The GRU updates its state \(\boldsymbol{s}_t\) based on the previous target word \(\boldsymbol{e}(y_t-1)\), the previous decoder state \(\boldsymbol{s}_{t-1}\), context vector \(\boldsymbol{c}_t\), and the emotional state update \(\boldsymbol{M}_{r,t}^I\):

\[\boldsymbol{s}_{\boldsymbol{t}}=\mathbf{G} \mathbf{R} \mathbf{U}\left(\boldsymbol{s}_{t-1},\left[\boldsymbol{c}_{t} ; \boldsymbol{e}\left(y_{t-1}\right) ; \boldsymbol{M}_{r, t}^{I}\right]\right)
\]

Based on this state, the word-generation distribution \(\boldsymbol{o}_t\) is obtained, and the next word \(y_t\) can be sampled. After the next word is generated, \(\boldsymbol{M}_{r,t+1}^I\) is written back into internal memory. Note that executing Equation 9 repeatedly is equivalent to multiplying matrices continuously, creating a decay effect because \(0≤ sigmoid(·) ≤ 1\). This is similar to the delete operation in memory networks.

In short, the internal memory part first computes read-gate parameters, multiplies them element-wise with internal memory, feeds the result into the GRU, computes the decoder state at current time `t`, and then writes through the write gate into internal memory. This causes decay because the write-gate values are numbers between 0 and 1.

### External memory

External memory decides whether to choose a general word or an emotional word, using a type selector.

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15591030/image-03.png)

Given current decoder state \(s_t\), the model computes emotional softmax \(P_e(y_t=w_e)\) from the emotional vocabulary read from external memory, and general softmax \(P_g(y_t=w_g)\) from the general vocabulary. The type selector \(α_t\) controls the weight for generating an emotional or general word \(y_t\). Finally, the next word is sampled from the next-word probability, which is the [concatenation](https://blog.csdn.net/Frank_LJiang/article/details/104333272) of two weighted probabilities. The process is formulated as Equations 11-14:

\[\begin{aligned}
\alpha_{t} &=\operatorname{sigmoid}\left(\mathbf{v}_{\mathbf{u}}{ }^{\top} s_{t}\right), \\
P_{g}\left(y_{t}=w_{g}\right) &=\operatorname{softmax}\left(\mathbf{W}_{\mathbf{g}}^{\mathrm{o}} s_{t}\right), \\
P_{e}\left(y_{t}=w_{e}\right) &=\operatorname{softmax}\left(\mathbf{W}_{\mathrm{e}}^{\mathrm{o}} s_{t}\right), \\
y_{t} \sim \boldsymbol{o}_{t}=P\left(y_{t}\right) &=\left[\begin{array}{r}
\left(1-\alpha_{t}\right) P_{g}\left(y_{t}=w_{g}\right) \\
\alpha_{t} P_{e}\left(y_{t}=w_{e}\right)
\end{array}\right],
\end{aligned}
\]

Here \(\alpha_t\in[0,1]\) is a scalar used to balance the choice between emotional word \(w_e\) and general word \(w_g\). \(P_g/P_e\) are the distributions of general words and emotional words respectively. \(P_{(y_t)}\) is the final word decoding distribution. Note that the two vocabularies do not overlap, and the final distribution \(P_{(y_t)}\) is the concatenation of the two distributions.

## Experiments

### Implementation details

The proposed model is implemented with TensorFlow.

The encoder and decoder use two-layer GRU structures, each layer containing 256 hidden units, with different parameter sets. Word embedding size is set to 100.

Vocabulary size is limited to 40,000. Emotion category embedding size is set to 100.

Internal memory is a trainable matrix of size `6×256`. External memory is a list of 40,000 words, including general words and emotional words, but emotional words have different marks.

To generate diverse responses, beam search is used during decoding. Beam size is set to 20. After removing responses containing unknown words, responses are re-ranked by generation probability.

The model uses mini-batch stochastic gradient descent, SGD. Batch size and learning rate are set to 128 and 0.5 respectively.

To speed up training, a seq2seq model with pretrained word embeddings is trained on the STC data set. Then the ECM model is trained on the ESTC data set, initialized with parameters from the pretrained seq2seq model.

The authors run 20 epochs. The training phase for each model takes about one week on a Titan X GPU machine.

### Baseline models

The baselines are a general seq2seq model and an emotion category embedding model, Emb, created by the authors. In Emb, the emotion category is embedded into a vector, and this vector is used as input at each decoding position, similar to user embedding. Since emotion category is a high-level abstraction of emotional expression, this is a suitable baseline for the model.

### Results

**Automatic evaluation**

Emotion accuracy is used to detect consistency between the expected emotion category, used as model input, and the predicted emotion category of the response generated by the emotion classifier.

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15591030/image-04.png)

ECM performs best in emotion accuracy. In [perplexity](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/44107044), it performs better than Seq2Seq but worse than Emb.

**Human evaluation**

Annotators are asked to score responses by content, with scores 0, 1, or 2, and emotion, with scores 0 or 1, and indicate which of the two systems they prefer.

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15591030/image-05.png)

ECM's response scores are significantly better than other methods on both metrics. As shown, 27.2% of ECM-generated responses have content score 2 and emotion score 1, while Emb and Seq2Seq only have 22.8% and 9.0%, respectively. This indicates that ECM can generate higher-quality responses in both content and emotion.

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15591030/image-06.png)

For all emotion categories, ECM performs better than other methods at the emotional level. However, ECM performs worse than baseline models at the content level for the Disgust and Angry categories, because these two categories do not have enough training data. For example, in the ESTC data set, the Angry category has only 234,635 responses, far fewer than other categories.

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15591030/image-07.png)

Emotion models, Emb and ECM, are preferred over Seq2Seq. ECM is more preferred because of explicit emotional expression and content appropriateness.

## Conclusion and future work

In this paper, the authors propose the Emotional Chatting Machine, ECM, to model emotional influence in large-scale dialogue generation. Three emotional-factor models are proposed: emotion category embedding, internal emotion memory, and external memory. Automatic and manual evaluations show that ECM can generate appropriate responses not only at the content level, but also at the emotional level.

In future work, the authors will explore the interaction between sentiment and ECM: the model should determine the most appropriate emotion category for the response, instead of receiving a specified emotion category. This may be challenging because the task depends on topic, context, or user emotion.
