# DialogueRNN: An Attentive RNN for Emotion Detection in Conversations

## Abstract

Emotion detection in conversations is an important step for many applications, including opinion mining over chat histories, social-media threads, debates, argument mining, and understanding customer feedback in live conversations. Current systems do not separately model each participant in a conversation by adapting to the speaker of each utterance. In this paper, the authors describe a new recurrent neural network (RNN)-based method that tracks the state of individual participants throughout a conversation and uses that information for emotion classification. The model shows significant advantages over state-of-the-art methods on two different datasets.

## 1 Introduction

Emotion detection in conversations has attracted increasing attention from the research community because of its use in many important tasks, such as opinion mining over chat histories and threads on social media platforms including YouTube, Facebook, and Twitter. A thread here refers to a series of replies to a single post. These replies can come from the original author or from other users, and they are displayed one after another under the original post. In this paper, the authors propose an RNN-based approach to address these needs by processing the large amount of conversation data now available.

Existing systems, including state-of-the-art methods, do not meaningfully distinguish between different participants in a conversation. They are not aware of who spoke a particular utterance. In contrast, as a conversation progresses, this model represents each participant with a party state based on the utterance, context, and the current state of that participant. The model is based on the assumption that three major aspects of a conversation are related to emotion: the speaker, the context from previous utterances, and the emotions of previous utterances. These three aspects are not necessarily independent, but modeling them separately brings a clear improvement over the state of the art, as shown in Table 2. In dyadic conversations, participants have different roles. Therefore, to extract context, it is crucial to consider the previous turns of both the speaker and the listener at a given moment, as shown in Figure 1.

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15557430/image-01.png)

The proposed DialogueRNN system uses three gated recurrent units (GRUs) to model these aspects. Incoming utterances are fed into two GRUs, called the global GRU and the party GRU, to update the context and participant states. The global GRU encodes utterances while also encoding information about the corresponding participant.

The global GRU provides a context representation that contains information from all previous utterances by different participants in the conversation. The speaker state depends on the context, obtained through an attention mechanism, and the speaker's previous state. This ensures that at time `t`, the speaker state receives information directly from the speaker's previous state and from the global GRU, which contains information about previous participants. Finally, the updated speaker state is fed into the emotion GRU to decode the emotion representation of the given utterance for emotion classification. At time `t`, the emotion GRU receives the emotion representation from `t - 1` and the speaker state at `t`.

The emotion GRU and global GRU play key roles in modeling relationships between participants. The party GRU, on the other hand, models the relationship between two consecutive states of the same participant. In DialogueRNN, all three types of GRUs are connected recurrently. The authors argue that DialogueRNN outperforms state-of-the-art contextual emotion classifiers because it provides better context representation.

The rest of the paper is organized as follows: Section 2 discusses related work; Section 3 describes the model in detail; Sections 4 and 5 present experimental results; Section 6 concludes the paper.

## 2 Related Work

Emotion recognition has drawn attention across natural language processing, psychology, cognitive science, and other fields (Picard 2010). Ekman (1993) found correlations between emotions and facial cues. Datcu and Rothkrantz (2008) fused audio information with visual cues for emotion recognition. Alm, Roth, and Sproat (2005) introduced text-based emotion recognition, later developed by Strapparava and Mihalcea (2010). Wollmer et al. (2010) used contextual information for emotion recognition in a multimodal setting. More recently, Poria et al. (2017) successfully applied RNN-based deep networks to multimodal emotion recognition, followed by other work (Chen et al. 2017; Zadeh et al. 2018a; 2018b).

Reproducing interpersonal interaction requires a deep understanding of dialogue. Ruusuvuori (2013) pointed out that emotion plays a key role in conversation. It has been argued that emotional dynamics in conversation are an interpersonal phenomenon (Richards, Butler, and Gross 2003). Therefore, this model explicitly incorporates interpersonal interaction. In addition, since conversations are naturally temporal, the model captures temporality through recurrent networks (Poria et al. 2017).

Memory networks (Sukhbaatar et al. 2015) have achieved success in several NLP fields, including question answering (Sukhbaatar et al. 2015; Kumar et al. 2016), machine translation (Bahdanau, Cho, and Bengio 2014), and speech recognition (Graves, Wayne, and Danihelka 2014). Hazarika et al. (2018) used memory networks for emotion recognition in dyadic conversations, where two separate memory networks modeled interaction between speakers and produced state-of-the-art performance.

# 3 Methodology

### 3.1 Problem Setup

Assume a conversation has \(M\) participants \(p_{1},p_{2},...,p_{m}\). In the datasets used by the authors, \(M=2\). The task is to predict emotion labels for utterances \(u_{1},u_{2},...,u_{N}\), where the labels include happy, sad, neutral, angry, excited, and frustrated. Utterance \(u_{t}\) is spoken by participant \(p_{s(u_{t})}\), where \(s\) maps an utterance to the index of its corresponding party. Similarly, \(u_{t} \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{m}}\) is the utterance representation obtained using the feature extractor described below.

### 3.2 Unimodal Feature Extraction

For a fair comparison with the state-of-the-art conversational memory network (CMN) by Hazarika et al. (2018), the authors follow the same feature extraction procedure.

**Text Feature Extraction**

A convolutional neural network (CNN) is used for text feature extraction. Following Kim (2014), three different convolution filters of sizes 3, 4, and 5 are used to obtain n-gram features from each utterance, with 50 feature maps for each filter. The outputs are then max-pooled and activated using a rectified linear unit (ReLU). These activations are concatenated and fed into a 100-dimensional dense layer, which is treated as the text utterance representation. The network is trained at the utterance level using emotion labels.

**Audio-Visual Feature Extraction**

As in Hazarika et al. (2018), 3D-CNN and openSMILE (Eyben, Wollmer, and Schuller 2010) are used for visual and acoustic feature extraction respectively.

### 3.3 Our Model

The model assumes that the emotion of an utterance in a conversation mainly depends on three factors:

- The speaker.

- The context provided by previous utterances.

- The emotions implied by the previous utterances.

The DialogueRNN model, shown in Figure 2a, models these factors as follows.

Each party is represented by a party state that changes when that party speaks. This allows the model to track the emotional dynamics of both sides through the conversation, as those dynamics are related to the emotion behind each utterance.

In addition, the context of utterances is modeled with a global state, called global because it is shared across parties. Previous utterances and party states are jointly encoded into this global state for context representation, which is necessary for accurate party-state representation.

Finally, the model infers an emotion representation from the speaker's party state and the previous speaker states as context. This emotion representation is used for final emotion classification.

GRU cells (Chung et al. 2014) are used to update states and representations. Each GRU computes the hidden state \(h_{t} = GRU_{*}(h_{t-1},x_{t})\), where \(x_{t}\) is the current input and \(h_{t-1}\) is the previous state of the GRU. \(h_{t}\) also serves as the output of the current GRU. Details of the GRU computation are provided in the supplementary material. GRUs are efficient networks with trainable parameters \(W_{*,\{h, x\}}^{\{r, z, c\}} \text { , } b_{\star}^{\{r, z, c\}}\).

The emotion representation of the current utterance is modeled as a function of the emotion representation of previous utterances and the current speaker state. Finally, this emotion representation is fed into a softmax layer for emotion classification.

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15557430/image-02.png)

**Global State (Global GRU)**

The goal of the global state is to capture the context of a given utterance by jointly encoding the utterance and speaker state. Each state also acts as a speaker-specific utterance representation. Attending to these states helps increase dependencies between speakers and between utterances, producing a better context representation. The current utterance \(u_{t}\) changes the speaker state from \(q_{s(u_{t}),t-1}\) to \(q_{s(u_{t}),t}\). This change is captured with a GRU cell \(GRU_{\mathcal{G}}\), whose output size is \(D_{\mathcal{G}}\), using \(u_{t}\) and \(q_{s(u_{t}),t-1}\):

\[\begin{equation}
g_{t}=G R U_{\mathcal{G}}\left(g_{t-1},\left(u_{t} \oplus q_{s\left(u_{t}\right), t-1}\right)\right),\tag{1}
\end{equation}
\]

Here, \(D_{\mathcal{G}}\) is the size of the global-state vector, \(D_{\mathcal{P}}\) is the size of the party-state vector, \(W_{\mathcal{G}, h}^{\{r, z, c\}} \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{\mathcal{G}} \times D_{\mathcal{G}}}\), \(W_{\mathcal{G}, x}^{\{r, z, c\}} \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{\mathcal{G}} \times\left(D_{m}+D_{\mathcal{P}}\right)}\), \(b_{\mathcal{G}}^{\{r, z, c\}} \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{\mathcal{G}}}\), \(q_{s\left(u_{t}\right), t-1} \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{\mathcal{P}}}\), and \(g_{t}, g_{t-1} \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{\mathcal{G}}}\). \(\oplus\) denotes concatenation.

Concatenation means appending a new vector after the original vector.

**Party State (Party GRU)**

DialogueRNN tracks the state of each speaker throughout the conversation using fixed-size vectors \(q_{1},q_{2},…,q_{M}\). These states represent the speaker's state in the conversation and are relevant to emotion classification. The states are updated according to the participant's current role at time `t`, speaker or listener, and the incoming utterance \(u_{t}\). For all participants, the state vectors are initialized as empty vectors. The main purpose of this module is to ensure that the model knows who speaks each utterance and processes it accordingly.

**Speaker Update (Speaker GRU)**

A speaker usually shapes a response according to the context, meaning the preceding utterances in the conversation. Therefore, the context \(c_{t}\) related to utterance \(u_{t}\) is captured as follows:

\[\alpha =\operatorname{softmax}\left(u_{t}^{T} W_{\alpha}\left[g_{1}, g_{2}, \ldots, g_{t-1}\right]\right),\tag{2}
\]

\[\operatorname{softmax}(x) =\left[e^{x_{1}} / \Sigma_{i} e^{x_{i}}, e^{x_{2}} / \Sigma_{i} e^{x_{i}}, \ldots\right],\tag{3}
\]

\[c_{t} =\alpha\left[g_{1}, g_{2}, \ldots, g_{t-1}\right]^{T},\tag{4}
\]

where \(g_{1}, g_{2}, \ldots, g_{t-1}\) are the previous \(t-1\) global states, \(g_{i} \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{\mathcal{G}}}\), \(W_{\alpha} \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{m} \times D_{\mathcal{G}}}\), \(\alpha^{T} \in \mathbb{R}^{(t-1)}\), and \(c_{t} \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{\mathcal{G}}}\). In the first equation above, the model computes attention scores \(α\) over the previous global states that represent previous utterances. This allows utterances that are emotionally relevant to \(u_{t}\) to receive higher attention scores. Finally, in the third equation, the context vector \(c_{t}\) is computed by pooling the previous global states with \(α\).

The GRU cell \(GRU_{\mathcal{P}}\), with output size \(D_{\mathcal{P}}\), updates the current speaker state from \(q_{s(u_{t}),t-1}\) to \(q_{s(u_{t}),t}\), based on the utterance \(u_{t}\) and context \(c_{t}\):

\[q_{s\left(u_{t}\right), t}=G R U_{\mathcal{P}}\left(q_{s\left(u_{t}\right), t-1},\left(u_{t} \oplus c_{t}\right)\right),\tag{5}
\]

where \(W_{\mathcal{P}, h}^{\{r, z, c\}} \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{\mathcal{P}} \times D_{\mathcal{P}}}\), \(W_{\mathcal{P}, x}^{\{r, z, c\}} \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{\mathcal{P}} \times\left(D_{m}+D_{\mathcal{G}}\right)}\), \(b_{\mathcal{P}}^{\{r, z, c\}} \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{\mathcal{P}}}\), and \(q_{s\left(u_{t}\right), t}, q_{s\left(u_{t}\right), t-1} \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{\mathcal{P}}}\). This encodes information from the current utterance and its context from the global GRU into the speaker state \(q_{s\left(u_{t}\right), t}\), which helps emotion classification.

**Listener Update**

The listener state models how a listener changes state because of the speaker's utterance. The authors tried two listener-state update mechanisms:

- Keep the listener state unchanged:

\[\forall i \neq s\left(u_{t}\right), q_{i, t}=q_{i, t-1},\tag{6}
\]

- Use another GRU cell \(GRU_{\mathcal{L}}\) to update the listener state based on listener visual cues, such as facial expression \(v_{i,t}\), and context \(c_{t}\):

\[\forall i \neq s\left(u_{t}\right), q_{i, t}=G R U_{\mathcal{L}}\left(q_{i, t-1},\left(v_{i, t} \oplus c_{t}\right)\right),\tag{7}
\]

where \(v_{i, t} \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{\mathcal{V}}}\), \(W_{\mathcal{L}, h}^{\{r, z, c\}} \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{\mathcal{P}} \times D_{\mathcal{P}}}\), \(W_{\mathcal{L}, x}^{\{r, z, c\}}   \mathbb{R}^{D_{\mathcal{P}} \times\left(D_{\mathcal{V}}+D_{\mathcal{G}}\right)}\), and \(b_{\mathcal{L}}^{\{r, z, c\}} \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{\mathcal{P}}}\). The listener visual feature \(v_{i,t}\) for participant `i` at time `t` is extracted using the model introduced by Arriaga, Valdenegro Toro, and Ploger (2017), pretrained on the FER2013 dataset, with feature size \(D_{V}=7\).

The simpler first method proved sufficient, because the second method produced very similar results while increasing the number of parameters. The reason is that a listener is relevant to the conversation only when he or she speaks. In other words, a silent participant does not affect the conversation. When a party speaks, the model updates that party's state \(q_{i}\) using context \(c_{t}\), which contains relevant information from all previous utterances. This makes explicit listener-state updates unnecessary, as shown in Table 2.

**Emotion Representation (Emotion GRU)**

The model infers the emotion-related representation \(e_{t}\) of utterance \(u_{t}\) from the speaker state \(q_{s(u_{t}),t}\) and the emotion representation \(e_{t-1}\) of previous utterances. Since context is important for the emotion of input utterance \(u_{t}\), \(e_{t-1}\) provides emotion-relevant contextual information from other party states \(q_{s(u_{＜t}),＜t}\) into the emotion representation \(e_{t}\). This builds a connection between the speaker and other participants. Therefore, a GRU cell \(GRU_{\mathcal{E}}\) of size \(D_{\mathcal{E}}\) is used to model \(e_{t}\):

\[e_{t}=G R U_{\mathcal{E}}\left(e_{t-1}, q_{s\left(u_{t}\right), t}\right),\tag{8}
\]

where \(D_{\mathcal{E}}\) is the size of the emotion-representation vector, \(e_{\{t, t-1\}} \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{\mathcal{E}}}\), \(W_{\mathcal{E}, h}^{\{r, z, c\}} \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{\mathcal{E}} \times D_{\mathcal{E}}}\), \(W_{\mathcal{E}, x}^{\{r, z, c\}} \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{\mathcal{E}} \times D_{\mathcal{P}}}\), and \(b_{\mathcal{E}}^{\{r, z, c\}} \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{\mathcal{E}}}\).

Because the speaker state obtains information from the global states and acts as a speaker-specific utterance representation, one might argue that the model already has access to information from other parties. However, as shown in the ablation study in Section 5.6, the emotion GRU improves performance by directly connecting previous participant states. The authors also view the speaker and global GRUs, \(GRU_{\mathcal{P}}\) and \(GRU_{\mathcal{G}}\), as jointly playing an encoder-like role, while the emotion GRU plays a decoder-like role.

**Emotion Classification**

A two-layer perceptron and final softmax layer are used to compute probabilities over \(c=6\) emotion classes from the emotion representation \(e_{t}\) of utterance \(u_{t}\). The most likely emotion class is selected:

\[l_{t} =\operatorname{ReLU}\left(W_{l} e_{t}+b_{l}\right),\tag{9}
\]

\[\mathcal{P}_{t} =\operatorname{softmax}\left(W_{\text {smax }} l_{t}+b_{\text {smax }}\right),\tag{10}
\]

\[\hat{y_{t}} =\underset{i}{\operatorname{argmax}}\left(\mathcal{P}_{t}[i]\right),\tag{11}
\]

where \(W_{l} \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{l} \times D_{\mathcal{E}}}\), \(b_{l} \in \mathbb{R}^{D_{l}}\), \(W_{\text {smax }} \in \mathbb{R}^{c \times D_{l}}\), \(b_{\text {smax }} \in   \mathbb{R}^{c}\), \(\mathcal{P}_{t} \in \mathbb{R}^{c}\), and \(\hat{y}_{t}\) is the predicted label of utterance \(u_{t}\).

**Training**

Categorical cross-entropy and L2 regularization are used as the loss function during training:

\[L=-\frac{1}{\sum_{s=1}^{N} c(s)} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_{j=1}^{c(i)} \log \mathcal{P}_{i, j}\left[y_{i, j}\right]+\lambda\|\theta\|_{2},\tag{12}
\]

where \(N\) is the number of samples or conversations, \(c(i)\) is the number of utterances in sample \(i\), \(\mathcal{P}_{i, j}\) is the probability distribution over emotion labels for utterance \(j\) in conversation \(i\), \(y_{i,j}\) is the expected classification label for utterance \(j\) in conversation \(i\), \(\lambda\) is the weight of L2 regularization, and \(\theta\) is the set of trainable parameters:

\[\begin{split}
\theta=\left\{W_{\alpha}, W_{\mathcal{P},\{h, x\}}^{\{r, z, c\}}, b_{\mathcal{P}}^{\{r, z, c\}}, W_{\mathcal{G},\{h, x\}}^{\{r, z, c\}}, b_{\mathcal{G}}^{\{r, z, c\}},\right.\\
&\\ \left. W_{\mathcal{E},\{h, x\}}^{\{r, z, c\}},b_{\mathcal{E}}^{\{r, z, c\}}, W_{l}, b_{l}, W_{s \max }, b_{s \max }\right\} .
\end{split}
\]

The network is trained with the Adam optimizer (Kingma and Ba 2014), which is based on stochastic gradient descent. Hyperparameters are optimized with grid search, and their values are provided in the supplementary material.

### 3.4 DialogueRNN Variants

DialogueRNN, described in Section 3.3, is used as the basis for the following models.

**DialogueRNN + Listener State Update (DialogueRNN)**

This variant updates listener states using the obtained speaker state \(q_{s(u_{t}),t}\), as described in Equation (7).

**Bidirectional DialogueRNN (BiDialogueRNN)**

Bidirectional DialogueRNN is similar to a bidirectional RNN, where two different RNNs process the input sequence in forward and backward directions. The RNN outputs are concatenated at the sequence level. Similarly, in BiDialogueRNN, the final emotion representation contains information from past and future utterances in the conversation through forward and backward dialogue passes, which provides better contextual information for emotion classification.

**DialogueRNN + Attention (DialogueRNN + Att)**

For each emotion representation \(e_{t}\), attention is applied to all surrounding emotion representations in the conversation by matching them with \(e_{t}\), as shown in Equations (13) and (14). This provides context from future and previous utterances based on attention scores.

**Bidirectional DialogueRNN + Emotional Attention (BiDialogueRNN + Att)**

For each emotion representation \(e_{t}\) in BiDialogueRNN, attention is applied to all emotion representations in the conversation, so context can be captured from other utterances:

\[\beta_{t}=\operatorname{softmax}\left(e_{t}^{T} W_{\beta}\left[e_{1}, e_{2}, \ldots, e_{N}\right]\right),\tag{13}
\]

\[\tilde{e}_{t}=\beta_{t}\left[e_{1}, e_{2}, \ldots, e_{N}\right]^{T},\tag{14}
\]

where \(e_{t} \in \mathbb{R}^{2 D_{\mathcal{E}}}\), \(W_{\beta} \in \mathbb{R}^{2 D_{\mathcal{E}} \times 2 D_{\mathcal{E}}}\), \(\tilde{e}_{t} \in \mathbb{R}^{2 D_{\mathcal{E}}}\), and \(\beta_{t}^{T} \in   \mathbb{R}^{N}\). As shown in Equations (9) to (11), \(\tilde{e}_{t}\) is then fed into a two-layer perceptron for emotion classification.

## 4 Experimental Setup

### 4.1 Datasets Used

The authors evaluate DialogueRNN on two emotion-detection datasets: IEMOCAP (Busso et al. 2008) and AVEC (Schuller et al. 2012). Both datasets are split into training and test sets at roughly an 80/20 ratio, and the split is speaker-independent. Table 1 shows the distribution of sequences and test samples for both datasets.

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15557430/image-03.png)

**IEMOCAP**

The IEMOCAP dataset (Busso et al. 2008) contains dyadic conversation videos from ten unique speakers. Only the first eight speakers from sessions 1 to 4 are used for training. Each video contains a monologue-style dyadic conversation divided into several utterances. Each utterance is annotated with one of six emotion labels: happy, sad, neutral, angry, excited, and frustrated.

**AVEC**

The AVEC dataset (Schuller et al. 2012) is a modification of the SEMAINE database (McKeown et al. 2012), which contains interactions between humans and artificial intelligent agents. Each utterance in the conversation has four real-valued affective attributes: valence \([−1, 1]\), arousal \([−1, 1]\), expectancy \([−1, 1]\), and power \([0, ∞)\). In the original database, annotations are available every 0.2 seconds. To adapt the annotations to utterance-level needs, the authors average the attributes over the duration of each utterance.

### 4.2 Baselines and State of the Art

To comprehensively evaluate DialogueRNN, the authors compare it with the following baseline methods.

**c-LSTM (Poria et al. 2017)**

A bidirectional LSTM (Hochreiter and Schmidhuber 1997) captures context from surrounding utterances to generate context-aware utterance representations. However, this model does not distinguish between speakers.

**c-LSTM + Att (Poria et al. 2017)**

In this variant, attention is applied to the c-LSTM output at each timestamp using Equations (13) and (14). This provides better contextual information for the final utterance representation.

**TFN (Zadeh et al. 2017)**

This is a special case for the multimodal setting. Tensor outer products are used to capture inter-modal and intra-modal interactions. The model does not capture contextual information from surrounding utterances.

**MFN (Zadeh et al. 2018a)**

This model is specific to the multimodal setting. It uses multi-view learning by modeling view-specific and cross-view interactions. Like TFN, this model does not use contextual information.

**CNN (Kim 2014)**

This is the same as the text feature extractor network described in Section 3.2. It does not use contextual information from surrounding utterances.

**Memnet (Sukhbaatar et al. 2015)**

As described by Hazarika et al. (2018), the current utterance is fed into a memory network whose memory corresponds to previous utterances. The output of the memory network is used as the final utterance representation for emotion classification.

**CMN (Hazarika et al. 2018)**

This state-of-the-art method uses two different GRUs to model utterance context in the dialogue history for two speakers. Finally, an utterance representation is obtained by providing the current utterance as a query to two different speaker memory networks.

### 4.3 Modalities

The model is mainly evaluated with text modality. However, to verify its effectiveness in multimodal settings, the authors also experiment with multimodal features.

## 5 Results and Discussion

DialogueRNN and its variants are compared with baselines on textual data in Table 2. As expected, DialogueRNN achieves better average performance than all baseline methods on both datasets, including the state-of-the-art CMN.

### 5.1 Comparison with the State of the Art

The authors compare DialogueRNN with the state-of-the-art CMN on IEMOCAP and AVEC in the text setting.

**IEMOCAP**

As shown in Table 2, on the IEMOCAP dataset, the model outperforms the state-of-the-art CMN method by 2.77% in accuracy and 3.76% in F1-score on average. The authors attribute this improvement to fundamental differences between CMN and DialogueRNN, mainly:

- Modeling party state with \(GRU_{\mathcal{P}}\) in Equation (5).

- Speaker-specific utterance processing in Equations (1) and (5).

- Capturing global state with \(GRU_{\mathcal{G}}\) in Equation (1).

The paper handles six imbalanced emotion labels and also investigates model performance on each individual label. DialogueRNN outperforms the state-of-the-art CMN method by a large margin in five of the six emotion categories. For the frustrated class, DialogueRNN's F1-score is 1.23% behind CMN. The authors believe that DialogueRNN could surpass CMN on frustrated if it used a separate classifier for that class. However, Table 2 shows that some DialogueRNN variants, such as BiDialogueRNN, already exceed CMN on the frustrated class.

**AVEC**

DialogueRNN outperforms CMN on valence, arousal, expectancy, and power, as shown in Table 2. This significantly reduces the mean absolute error (MAE) and Pearson correlation coefficient \(r\) averaged over the four attributes. The authors believe this is due to the inclusion of party state and the emotion GRU, which are missing from CMN.

### 5.2 DialogueRNN and DialogueRNN Variants

The paper discusses the performance of different DialogueRNN variants on the text modality for IEMOCAP and AVEC.

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15557430/image-04.png)

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15557430/image-05.png)

\(DialogueRNN_{l}\)

According to Table 2, explicit listener-state updates perform slightly worse than regular DialogueRNN on both IEMOCAP and AVEC. The only exception is the happy label in IEMOCAP, where \(DialogueRNN_{l}\) has an F1-score 1.71% higher than DialogueRNN. The authors speculate that a listener is only relevant to the conversation when he or she speaks. In DialogueRNN, when a party speaks, the model updates that party's state \(q_{i}\) using context \(c_{t}\), which already contains relevant information from all previous utterances. Therefore, explicit listener-state updates in \(DialogueRNN_{l}\) are unnecessary.

**BiDialogueRNN**

Because BiDialogueRNN captures contextual information from future utterances, it is expected to perform better than DialogueRNN. Table 2 confirms this: BiDialogueRNN achieves better average performance than DialogueRNN on both datasets.

**DialogueRNN + Attn**

DialogueRNN + Attn also uses information from future utterances. In this model, the information is obtained by matching past and future utterances with the current utterance and computing attention scores. This gives higher relevance to emotionally important contextual utterances and results in better performance than BiDialogueRNN. Compared with BiDialogueRNN, it improves the F1-score by 1.23% on IEMOCAP and consistently lowers MAE while increasing \(r\) on AVEC.

**BiDialogueRNN + Attn**

Because this setting uses emotion representations from BiDialogueRNN to generate the final emotion representation, it is expected to perform better than both BiDialogueRNN and DialogueRNN + Attn. Table 2 confirms this. On both datasets, this model generally outperforms all other methods under discussion. On IEMOCAP, its average F1-score is 6.62% higher than the state-of-the-art CMN and 2.86% higher than regular DialogueRNN. On AVEC, it achieves the best performance across all four attributes.

### 5.3 Multimodal Setting

Since both IEMOCAP and AVEC contain multimodal information, the authors evaluate DialogueRNN using the multimodal features used and provided by Hazarika et al. (2018). Following Hazarika et al. (2018), they concatenate unimodal features as the fusion method, but fusion is not the focus of this paper. As shown in Table 3, DialogueRNN significantly outperforms strong baselines and the state-of-the-art CMN.

### 5.4 Case Study

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15557430/image-06.png)

**Dependence on Previous Utterances (DialogueRNN)**

One key component of DialogueRNN is the attention module over outputs from the global GRU \(GRU_{\mathcal{G}}\). Figure 3b compares the attention vector from the CMN model with the \(α\) attention vector from Equation (2) on a given test utterance. Compared with CMN, the attention in this model is more focused. CMN dilutes attention scores and makes a wrong classification. The authors observe this focused-attention trend and hypothesize that it can be interpreted as a confidence indicator. In this example, the emotion implied by \(P_{A}\)'s test utterance, turn 44, changes from neutral to frustrated. DialogueRNN correctly predicts this by attending to turns 41 and 42 spoken by \(P_{A}\) and \(P_{B}\) respectively. These two utterances provide self and inter-participant influences that trigger the emotional shift. CMN fails to capture this dependency and incorrectly predicts neutral.

**Dependence on Future Utterances (BiDialogueRNN + Att)**

Figure 3a visualizes the \(β\) attention from Equation (13) over emotion representations \(e_{t}\) in a segment of a conversation between a couple. In the discussion, the female participant \(P_{A}\) is initially neutral, while the male participant \(P_{B}\) remains angry. The figure shows that the female participant's emotional attention focuses on the duration of her neutral state, approximately turns 1 to 16. For example, turns 5, 7, and 8 strongly attend to turn 8. Interestingly, turn 5 attends to both a past utterance, turn 3, and a future utterance, turn 8. Similar trends in other utterances establish interdependence between future and past emotional states. The useful consideration of future utterances through \(GRU_{\mathcal{E}}\) is also evident in turns 6 and 9. These utterances focus on the distant future, turns 49 and 50, where the man is in an angry state, capturing emotional associations across time. Although the model misclassifies turn 6, it still infers a related emotion state, anger, from the correct state, frustrated. The paper analyzes this trend further in Section 5.5.

**Dependence on Distant Context**

For all correct predictions in the IEMOCAP test set shown in Figure 3d, the authors summarize the distribution of relative distances between a test utterance and the second-highest attended utterance, whether in the past or future. The result shows a decreasing trend, with the highest dependency in local context. However, a substantial portion of test utterances, around 18%, attend to utterances 20 to 40 turns away, highlighting the importance of long-term emotional dependencies. This mainly occurs in conversations that maintain a particular emotional tone and do not frequently shift emotions. Figure 3c shows a case of long-range contextual dependency. The presented conversation maintains a happy emotion throughout. Although turn 34 says, "Horrible thing. I hated it.", which appears negative, in the global context it expresses the speaker's current excitement. To disambiguate this case, the model attends to distant previous utterances, turns 11 and 14, treating them as prototypes for the emotional tone of the whole conversation.

### 5.5 Error Analysis

One notable trend in the predictions is high cross-prediction between related emotions. Most misclassifications of happy correspond to the excited class. In addition, anger and frustrated are often misclassified as each other. The authors suspect this is due to subtle differences between these emotion pairs, making disambiguation harder. Another class with a high error rate is neutral. The main reason may be that neutral is the majority class in the label distribution and dominates the other emotions.

At the conversation level, the authors observe many errors when the current turn is a point where the emotion of the same party changes. Among all such emotion changes in the test set, the model correctly predicts 47.5% of cases. This is lower than the 69.2% success rate when there is no emotion change. Emotional change in conversation is a complex phenomenon governed by underlying interactions. Further improvement on these cases remains an open research area.

### 5.6 Ablation Study

The main innovations of this method are the introduction of party state and the emotion GRU \(GRU_{\mathcal{E}}\). To study the effect of these two components comprehensively, the authors remove one at a time and evaluate their impact on IEMOCAP.

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15557430/image-07.png)

As expected, Table 4 shows that party state is very important. Without it, performance drops by 4.33%. The authors suspect that party state helps extract useful contextual information related to participants' emotions. The emotion GRU also has an effect, though smaller than party state; removing it causes a 2.51% performance drop. The likely reason is the lack of emotion representations from previous utterances in the contextual flow from other participants.

## 6 Conclusion

The paper proposes an RNN-based neural architecture for emotion detection in conversations. Compared with the state-of-the-art CMN method, this approach considers each incoming utterance together with speaker characteristics, providing a clearer context for the utterance. The model outperforms the current state of the art on both text and multimodal settings across two different datasets. The method is designed to scale to multi-party communication scenarios with more than two speakers, which the authors plan to explore in future work.
