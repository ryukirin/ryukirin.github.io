# Watermelon Book | Datawhale October (2)

## The three elements of machine learning

- Model: determine the hypothesis space according to the concrete problem.

- Strategy: determine the strategy for selecting the optimal model according to the evaluation criterion. This usually produces a loss function.

- Algorithm: solve the loss function and determine the optimal model.

## Linear models

### Basic form

Given an instance \(\boldsymbol{x}=(x_{1};x_{2};…;x_{d})\) described by \(d\) attributes, where \(x_i\) is the value of \(\boldsymbol{x}\) on the i-th attribute, a linear model tries to learn a function that makes predictions through a linear combination of attributes:

\[f(\boldsymbol{x})=w_{1}x_{1}+w_{2}x_{2}+…+w_{d}x_{d}+b
\]

In vector form:

\[f(\boldsymbol{x} )=\boldsymbol{w}^{T}\boldsymbol{x}+b
\]

where \(\boldsymbol{w}=(w_{1};w_{2};…;w_{d})\). Once \(\boldsymbol{w}\) and \(b\) are learned, the model is determined.

### Linear regression

#### Univariate linear regression

The goal is to learn a linear model that predicts real-valued output labels as accurately as possible:

\[f(x_{i})=wx_{i}+b,使得f(x_{i})\simeq y_{i}
\]

The key to determining \(w\) and \(b\) is how to measure the difference between \(f(x_i)\) and \(y\). Here we use mean squared error, the most common performance measure in regression tasks:

\[\begin{aligned}
(w^{*},b^{*})
& = \mathop{\arg\min}\limits_{(w,b)}\sum_{i=1}^{m} (f(x_{i})-y_{i})\\
& = \mathop{\arg\min}\limits_{(w,b)}\sum_{i=1}^{m} (y_{i}-wx_{i}-b)
\end{aligned}
\]

\(\mathop{\arg\min}\limits_{(w,b)}\) means the values of `w` and `b` that minimize the expression after it.

Mean squared error corresponds to Euclidean distance. The method of solving a model by minimizing mean squared error is called the least squares method. In linear regression, this means:

- Try to find a straight line that minimizes the sum of Euclidean distances from all samples to the line.

Least-squares parameter estimation for a linear regression model:

- Solve \(w\) and \(b\) so that \(E_{(w,b)}={\textstyle \sum_{i=1}^{m}}(y_{i}-wx_{i}-b)^{2}\) is minimized.

Solving for \(w\) and \(b\) is essentially an optimization problem for a multivariate function.

By differentiation:

\[\frac{\partial E(w,b)}{\partial w}=2(w\sum_{i=1}^{m} x_{i}^{2}-\sum_{i=1}^{m} (y_{i}-b)x_{i})
\]

\[\frac{\partial E(w,b)}{\partial b}=2(mb-\sum_{i=1}^{m} (y_{i}-wx_{i}))
\]

Setting the two expressions above to 0 gives the following. See the Pumpkin Book for the detailed derivation.

\[w = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{m} y_{i}(x_{i}-\bar{x} )}{\sum _{i=1}^{m}x_{i}^{2}-\frac{1}{2}(\sum_{i=1}^{m}x_{i} )^{2} }
\]

\[b = \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^{m} (y_{i}-wx_{i})
\]

#### Multivariate linear regression

For the basic form \(f(\boldsymbol{x} )=\boldsymbol{w}^{T}\boldsymbol{x}+b\), if it makes \(f(\boldsymbol{x}_{i})\simeq y_{i}\), it is called multivariate linear regression.

Represent \(w\) and \(b\) as \(\hat{\boldsymbol{w}}=(\boldsymbol{w};b)\), and represent data set `D` as an `m x (d+1)` matrix \(\mathbf{X}\). Each row corresponds to one example. The first `d` elements of the row correspond to the `d` attributes, and the last element is 1:

\[\mathbf{X} =\begin{pmatrix}
 x_{11} & x_{12} & \cdots & x_{1d} & 1\\
 x_{21} & x_{22} & \cdots & x_{2d} & 1\\
 \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots\\
 x_{m1} & x_{m2} & \cdots & x_{md} & 1\\
\end{pmatrix}
=\begin{pmatrix}
 x_{1}^{T} & 1\\
 x_{2}^{T} & 1\\
 \vdots & \vdots\\
 x_{m}^{T} & 1\\
\end{pmatrix}
\]

Let \(\boldsymbol{y}=(y_{1};y_{2};...;y_{m})\), then:

\[\hat{\boldsymbol{w}}^{*}=\underset{\hat{\boldsymbol{w}}}{\arg \min }(\boldsymbol{y}-\mathbf{X} \hat{\boldsymbol{w}})^{\mathrm{T}}(\boldsymbol{y}-\mathbf{X} \hat{\boldsymbol{w}})
\]

Let \(E_{\hat{\boldsymbol{w}}}=(\boldsymbol{y}-\mathbf{X} \hat{\boldsymbol{w}})^{\mathrm{T}}(\boldsymbol{y}-\mathbf{X} \hat{\boldsymbol{w}})\). Differentiating with respect to \(\hat{\boldsymbol{w}}\) gives:

\[\frac{\partial E_{\hat{\boldsymbol{w}}}}{\partial \hat{\boldsymbol{w}}}=2 \mathbf{X}^{\mathrm{T}}(\mathbf{X} \hat{\boldsymbol{w}}-\boldsymbol{y})
\]

When \(\mathbf{X}^{T}\mathbf{X}\) is full-rank or positive definite, setting the expression above to 0 gives:

\[\hat{\boldsymbol{w}}^{*}=(\mathbf{X}^{T}\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^{T}\boldsymbol{y}
\]

Let \(\hat{\boldsymbol{x}}_{i}=(\boldsymbol{x}_{i};1)\). The final learned multivariate linear regression model is:

\[f(\hat{\boldsymbol{x}}_{i})=\hat{\boldsymbol{x}}_{i}^{T}(\mathbf{X}^{T}\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^{T}\boldsymbol{y}
\]

When \(\mathbf{X}^{T}\mathbf{X}\) is not full-rank or positive definite, multiple \(\hat{\boldsymbol{w}}\) solutions may exist. Which one is selected as the output is determined by the inductive bias of the learning algorithm. A common approach is to introduce a regularization term.

### Logistic regression

It is actually used for classification. A mapping function is applied on top of a linear model to implement classification.

#### Binary classification

We need the probability `P` of the positive class, while the probability of the negative class is `1-P`. This probability satisfies `P∈[0,1]`. However, the predicted value \(f(x)\) in linear regression is in `R`, so a mapping function, the logistic function \(y=\frac{1}{1-e^{-z}}\), is applied to control the final output within `[0,1]`, where `z` is `f(x)`.

Then:

\[y=\frac{1}{1-e^{-(\boldsymbol{w}^{T}\boldsymbol{x}+b)}}
\]

After transformation:

\[\ln{\frac{y}{1-y}}=\boldsymbol{w}^{T}\boldsymbol{x}+b
\]

Treating \(y\) as \(p(y=1 | \boldsymbol{x})\), \(1-y\) is \(p(y=0 | \boldsymbol{x})\):

\[\ln{\frac{p(y=1 | \boldsymbol{x})}{p(y=0 | \boldsymbol{x})}}=\boldsymbol{w}^{T}\boldsymbol{x}+b
\]

This leads to:

\[p(y=1 | \boldsymbol{x}) =\frac{e^{\boldsymbol{w}^{T}\boldsymbol{x}+b}}{1+e^{\boldsymbol{w}^{T}\boldsymbol{x}+b}}
\]

\[p(y=0 | \boldsymbol{x}) =\frac{1}{1+e^{\boldsymbol{w}^{T}\boldsymbol{x}+b}}
\]

For convenience, let \(\beta =(\boldsymbol{w};b),\hat{\boldsymbol{x}}=(\boldsymbol{x};1)\). The expressions can be shortened to:

\[p(y=1 | \boldsymbol{x}) =\frac{e^{\beta^{T}\hat{\boldsymbol{x}}}}{1+e^{\beta^{T}\hat{\boldsymbol{x}}}}=p_{1}(\hat{\boldsymbol{x}};\beta)
\]

\[p(y=0 | \boldsymbol{x}) =\frac{1}{1+e^{\beta^{T}\hat{\boldsymbol{x}}}}=p_{0}(\hat{\boldsymbol{x}};\beta)
\]

Therefore, the probability mass function for \(y\in \{0,1\}\) is:

\[p(y|\hat{\boldsymbol{x}};\beta)=y\cdot p_{1}(\hat{\boldsymbol{x}};\beta)+(1-y)\cdot p_{0}(\hat{\boldsymbol{x}};\beta)
\]

*When `y = 1`, \(p(y|\hat{\boldsymbol{x}};\beta)=p_{1}(\hat{\boldsymbol{x}};\beta)\). When `y = 0`, \(p(y|\hat{\boldsymbol{x}};\beta)=p_{0}(\hat{\boldsymbol{x}};\beta)\).*

Logistic regression maximizes the log-likelihood:

\[\ell(\boldsymbol{\beta})=\sum_{i=1}^{m} \ln p(y_{i}|\hat{\boldsymbol{x}};\beta)
\]

Substituting into it gives:

\[\ell(\boldsymbol{\beta})=\sum_{i=1}^{m}\left(-y_{i} \boldsymbol{\beta}^{\mathrm{T}} \hat{\boldsymbol{x}}_{i}+\ln \left(1+e^{\boldsymbol{\beta}^{\mathrm{T}} \hat{\boldsymbol{x}}_i}\right)\right)\tag{1}
\]

*This is easier to derive by following the [Pumpkin Book](https://datawhalechina.github.io/pumpkin-book/#/chapter3/chapter3?id=_327).*

This expression is a higher-order differentiable continuous convex function with respect to \(\beta\). The optimal solution can be obtained by gradient descent or Newton's method.

The following sections record related knowledge for obtaining Equation (1).

##### Maximum likelihood estimation

[Understanding maximum likelihood estimation in one article](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/26614750)

For the function \(p(x|\theta)\), there are two inputs: `x`, which represents one concrete data point, and \(\theta\), which represents model parameters.

If \(\theta\) is known and fixed while `x` is variable, this function is called a probability function. It describes the probability of different sample points `x`.

If `x` is known and fixed while \(\theta\) is variable, this function is called a likelihood function. It describes the probability of observing the sample point `x` under different model parameters. Maximizing this probability is maximum likelihood estimation.

Maximum likelihood estimation can be understood as reverse inference. In most cases, we infer results from known conditions. In maximum likelihood estimation, the result is already known, and we search for the condition that makes this result most likely, using it as the estimate.

**General steps for finding a maximum likelihood estimate:**

(1) Write the likelihood function.

(2) Take the logarithm of the likelihood function and simplify.

(3) Differentiate, set the derivative to 0, and obtain the likelihood equation.

(4) Solve the likelihood equation. The obtained parameters are the answer. [[1]](https://www.cnblogs.com/wakarimasu#fn1)

##### Information theory

Information theory uses probability theory and stochastic processes as basic research tools to study the whole process of generalized communication systems.

**Self-information**

\[I(X)=-\log _{b} p(x)
\]

When `b = 2`, the unit is bit. When `b = e`, the unit is nat.

**Information entropy: expectation of self-information**

It measures uncertainty of random variable `X`. The larger the entropy, the higher the uncertainty.

**Relative entropy: KL divergence**

It measures the difference between two distributions. A typical use case is measuring the difference between an ideal distribution `p(x)` and a simulated distribution `q(x)`.

\[\begin{aligned}
D_{K L}(p \| q) &=\sum_{x} p(x) \log _{b}\left(\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}\right) \\
&=\sum_{x} p(x)\left(\log _{b} p(x)-\log _{b} q(x)\right) \\
&=\sum_{x} p(x) \log _{b} p(x)-\sum_{x} p(x) \log _{b} q(x)
\end{aligned}
\]

Here \(-\sum_{x} p(x) \log _{b} q(x)\) is called cross entropy.

The simulated distribution closest to the ideal distribution is the optimal distribution. It can be obtained by minimizing relative entropy, which is equivalent to minimizing cross entropy.

#### The three machine-learning elements of logistic regression

- Model: a linear model whose output range is `[0,1]`, using a monotonic differentiable function that approximates a step function.

- Strategy: maximum likelihood estimation and information theory.

- Algorithm: gradient descent and Newton's method.

### Linear discriminant analysis

The idea of linear discriminant analysis (LDA): given a training set, project samples onto a line so that projected points from the same class are as close as possible, while projected points from different classes are as far apart as possible.

From a geometric perspective, after projecting all training samples:

- Centers of different-class samples should be as far apart as possible.

- Variance of same-class samples should be as small as possible.

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15415213/image-01.png)

Given data set \(D=\{(x_{i},y_{i})\}_{i=1}^{m},y\in \{0,1\}\), let \(X_{i}、\mu _{i}、\Sigma _{i}\) respectively denote the set of examples, mean vector, and covariance matrix of class \(i\in \{0,1\}\). Here the subscript `i` in these three terms refers to 0 or 1; the subscript `i` in the data set denotes sample index. If the data is projected onto line \(w\), then the projected centers of the two classes on the line are, non-rigorously, \(w^{T}\mu _{0}\) and \(w^{T}\mu _{1}\). If all sample points are projected onto the line, the covariance of the two classes becomes \(w^{T}\Sigma _{0}w\) and \(w^{T}\Sigma _{1}w\). Since the line is one-dimensional, all four values above are real numbers.

- \(w^{T}\mu _{0}=|\boldsymbol{w}| \cdot\left|\boldsymbol{\mu}_{0}\right| \cdot \cos \theta_{0}\), where \(\left|\boldsymbol{\mu}_{0}\right| \cdot \cos \theta_{0}\) is the projection.

Thus, making different-class centers as far apart as possible, using this non-rigorous projection view, gives:

\[\max \left\|w^{T}\mu _{0}-w^{T}\mu _{1}\right\|_{2}^{2}
\]

\[\max \left\||\boldsymbol{w}| \cdot\left|\boldsymbol{\mu}_{0}\right| \cdot \cos \theta_{0}-|\boldsymbol{w}| \cdot\left|\boldsymbol{\mu}_{1}\right| \cdot \cos \theta_{1}\right\|_{2}^{2}
\]

Making same-class variance as small as possible, again in this non-rigorous variance view, gives:

\[\min\boldsymbol{w}^{\mathrm{T}} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{0} \boldsymbol{w}
\]

\[\begin{aligned}
\boldsymbol{w}^{\mathrm{T}} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{0} \boldsymbol{w} &=\boldsymbol{w}^{\mathrm{T}}\left(\sum_{\boldsymbol{x} \in X_{0}}\left(\boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_{0}\right)\left(\boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_{0}\right)^{\mathrm{T}}\right) \boldsymbol{w} \\
&=\sum_{\boldsymbol{x} \in X_{0}}\left(\boldsymbol{w}^{\mathrm{T}} \boldsymbol{x}-\boldsymbol{w}^{\mathrm{T}} \boldsymbol{\mu}_{0}\right)\left(\boldsymbol{x}^{\mathrm{T}} \boldsymbol{w}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_{0}^{\mathrm{T}} \boldsymbol{w}\right)
\end{aligned}
\]

Here \(w^{T}x\) is the sample, and \(w^{T}\mu_{0}\) is the center.

Considering both conditions gives:

\[\begin{aligned}
\max J
&=\frac{\left\|\boldsymbol{w}^{\mathrm{T}} \boldsymbol{\mu}_{0}-\boldsymbol{w}^{\mathrm{T}} \boldsymbol{\mu}_{1}\right\|_{2}^{2}}{\boldsymbol{w}^{\mathrm{T}} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{0} \boldsymbol{w}+\boldsymbol{w}^{\mathrm{T}} \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{1} \boldsymbol{w}}\\
&=\frac{\boldsymbol{w}^{\mathrm{T}}\left(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{0}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_{1}\right)\left(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{0}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_{1}\right)^{\mathrm{T}} \boldsymbol{w}}{\boldsymbol{w}^{\mathrm{T}}\left(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{0}+\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{1}\right) \boldsymbol{w}}
\end{aligned}
\]

Let \(\left(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{0}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_{1}\right)\left(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{0}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_{1}\right)^{T}\) be \(S_{b}\), the between-class scatter matrix, and let \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{0}+\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{1}\) be \(S_{w}\), the within-class scatter matrix. The expression becomes:

\[\max J=\frac{\boldsymbol{w}^{\mathrm{T}} \mathbf{S}_{b} \boldsymbol{w}}{\boldsymbol{w}^{\mathrm{T}} \mathbf{S}_{w} \boldsymbol{w}}
\]

This is the generalized Rayleigh quotient of \(S_b\) and \(S_w\).

The scale of \(w\) does not affect the result, so fix the denominator by setting \(\boldsymbol{w}^{\mathrm{T}} \mathbf{S}_{w} \boldsymbol{w}=1\). Any other term containing \(w\) could also be fixed. Then:

\[\begin{array}{ll}
\min _{w} & -\boldsymbol{w}^{\mathrm{T}} \mathbf{S}_{b} \boldsymbol{w} \\
\text { s.t. } & \boldsymbol{w}^{\mathrm{T}} \mathbf{S}_{w} \boldsymbol{w}=1
\end{array}
\]

By the Lagrange multiplier method, this is equivalent to:

\[\begin{array}{c}
\mathbf{S}_{b} \boldsymbol{w}=\lambda \mathbf{S}_{w} \boldsymbol{w} \\
\left(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{0}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_{1}\right)\left(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{0}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_{1}\right)^{\mathrm{T}} \boldsymbol{w}=\lambda \mathbf{S}_{w} \boldsymbol{w}
\end{array}
\]

If \(\left(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{0}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_{1}\right)\left(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{0}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_{1}\right)^{\mathrm{T}} \boldsymbol{w}=\gamma\), then:

\[\begin{array}{l}
\gamma\left(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{0}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_{1}\right)=\lambda \mathbf{S}_{w} \boldsymbol{w} \\
\boldsymbol{w}=\frac{\gamma}{\lambda} \mathbf{S}_{w}^{-1}\left(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{0}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_{1}\right)
\end{array}
\]

Because the final solution for \(w\) only cares about direction, not magnitude, \(\frac{\gamma}{\lambda}\) can take any value. If it is set to 1:

\[\boldsymbol{w}=\mathbf{S}_{w}^{-1}\left(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{0}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_{1}\right)
\]

**Covariance**

\[\sigma(x, y)=\frac{1}{n-1} \sum_{a}^{b}\left(x_{i}-\bar{x}\right)\left(y_{i}-\bar{y}\right)
\]

The coefficient in front can be omitted:

\[\sigma(x, y)=\sum_{a}^{b}\left(x_{i}-\bar{x}\right)\left(y_{i}-\bar{y}\right)
\]

**Norms**

- L1 norm: the sum of absolute values of all elements, \(||x||_{1}\)

- L2 norm: the magnitude of a vector, \(||x||_{2}\)

- Infinity norm:

Positive infinity norm: the smallest absolute value among all elements, \(||x||_{+\infty }=\min(|x_1|...|x_{n}|)\)

- Negative infinity norm: the largest absolute value among all elements, \(||x||_{-\infty }=\max(|x_1|...|x_{n}|)\)

**Lagrange multiplier method**

For an optimization problem that only has equality constraints, a maximization problem can be converted to a minimization problem by adding a negative sign:

\[\begin{array}{cl}
\min _{x} & f(\boldsymbol{x}) \\
\text { s.t. } & h_{i}(\boldsymbol{x})=0 \quad i=1,2, \ldots, n
\end{array}
\]

where \(\boldsymbol{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{n}, f(\boldsymbol{x}) \text { and } h_{i}(\boldsymbol{x})\) both have continuous first-order partial derivatives. First, write the Lagrangian:

\[L(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{\lambda})=f(\boldsymbol{x})+\sum_{i=1}^{n} \lambda_{i} h_{i}(\boldsymbol{x})
\]

where \( \boldsymbol{\lambda}=\left(\lambda_{1}, \lambda_{2}, \ldots, \lambda_{n}\right)^{\mathrm{T}}\) is the Lagrange multiplier. Then take the partial derivative of the Lagrangian with respect to \(\boldsymbol{x}\), set the derivative to 0, and solve it together with the constraint \(h_i(\boldsymbol{x})=0\). All solved \(\boldsymbol{x}\) values are possible extremum points for the optimization problem above.

**Generalized eigenvalues**

Let \(\mathbf{A}, \mathbf{B}\) be n-order square matrices. If there exists a number \(\lambda\) such that \(A  \boldsymbol{x}=\lambda \mathbf{B} \boldsymbol{x}\) has a nonzero solution, then \(\lambda\) is called a generalized eigenvalue of \(\mathbf{A}\) with respect to \(\mathbf{B}\), and \(\boldsymbol{x}\) is the eigenvector of \(\mathbf{A}\) with respect to \(\mathbf{B}\) corresponding to generalized eigenvalue \(\lambda\). In particular, when \(\mathbf{B}=\mathbf{I}\), the identity matrix, the generalized eigenvalue problem degenerates into the standard eigenvalue problem.

**Generalized Rayleigh quotient**

Let \(\mathbf{A}, \mathbf{B}\) be n-order Hermitian matrices. A real symmetric matrix is equal to its transpose, while a Hermitian matrix generalizes this idea: it is equal to its conjugate transpose. If \(\mathbf{B}\) is positive definite, then \(R(\boldsymbol{x})=\frac{\boldsymbol{x}^{\mathrm{H}} \mathbf{A} \boldsymbol{x}}{\boldsymbol{x}^{\mathrm{H}} \mathbf{B} \boldsymbol{x}}(\boldsymbol{x} \neq \mathbf{0})\) is called the generalized Rayleigh quotient of \(\mathbf{A}\) with respect to \(\mathbf{B}\). In particular, when \(\mathbf{B}=\mathbf{I}\), the identity matrix, the generalized Rayleigh quotient degenerates into the Rayleigh quotient.

**Property of the generalized Rayleigh quotient**

Let \(\lambda_{i}, \boldsymbol{x}_{i}(i=1,2, \ldots, n)\) be generalized eigenvalues and eigenvectors of \(\mathbf{A}\) with respect to \(\mathbf{B}\), and \(\lambda_{1} \leqslant \lambda_{2} \leqslant \ldots \leqslant \lambda_{n}\).

\[\begin{array}{l}
\min _{\boldsymbol{x} \neq 0} R(\boldsymbol{x})=\frac{\boldsymbol{x}^{\mathrm{H}} \mathbf{A} \boldsymbol{x}}{\boldsymbol{x}^{\mathrm{H}} \mathbf{B} \boldsymbol{x}}=\lambda_{1}, \boldsymbol{x}^{*}=\boldsymbol{x}_{1} \\
\max _{\boldsymbol{x} \neq 0} R(\boldsymbol{x})=\frac{\boldsymbol{x}^{\mathrm{H}} \mathbf{A} \boldsymbol{x}}{\boldsymbol{x}^{\mathrm{H}} \mathbf{B} \boldsymbol{x}}=\lambda_{n}, \boldsymbol{x}^{*}=\boldsymbol{x}_{n}
\end{array}
\]

Proof: when \(\boldsymbol{x}^{\mathrm{H}} \mathbf{B} \boldsymbol{x}=1\) is fixed, the Lagrange multiplier method leads to a generalized eigenvalue problem \(\mathbf{A} \boldsymbol{x}=\lambda \mathbf{B} \boldsymbol{x}\). Therefore, all possible solutions for \(\boldsymbol{x}\) are the `n` generalized eigenvectors \(\boldsymbol{x}_{i}(i=1,2, \ldots, n)\). Substituting them into \(R(\boldsymbol{x})\) gives the conclusion above.
