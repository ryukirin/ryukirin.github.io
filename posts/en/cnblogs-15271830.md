# DialogueCRN: Contextual Reasoning Networks for Emotion Recognition in Conversations

Authors: Dou Hu, Lingwei Wei, Xiaoyong Huai

Original paper: [DialogueCRN: Contextual Reasoning Networks for Emotion Recognition in Conversations](https://aclanthology.org/2021.acl-long.547.pdf)

## Abstract

Emotion recognition in conversations (ERC) has become increasingly important for building empathetic machines. Recently, many methods have tried to mine dialogue context with deep learning models. However, these methods still have weaknesses in context understanding because they lack the ability to extract and integrate emotional clues. This paper proposes a new Contextual Reasoning Network, DialogueCRN, to fully understand dialogue context from a cognitive perspective. Inspired by cognitive theory of emotion, the authors design a multi-turn reasoning module to extract and integrate emotional clues. The reasoning module iteratively performs an intuitive retrieval process and a conscious reasoning process, imitating a distinctive form of human cognitive thinking.

## 1 Introduction

Emotion recognition in conversations (ERC) aims to detect the emotion of the speaker in each utterance of a dialogue. This is an important task for many areas related to empathetic machines (Zhou et al., 2020), including social opinion mining (Kumar et al., 2015), intelligent assistants (Konig et al., 2016), and healthcare (Pujol et al., 2019).

A dialogue usually contains contextual clues that trigger the emotion of the current utterance (Poria et al., 2019), such as causes or situations. Recent context-based studies for ERC (Poria et al., 2017; Hazarika et al., 2018b; Majumder et al., 2019) focus on perceiving context at the situational level or speaker level with deep learning models. However, these methods are not very effective at understanding context that contains rich emotional clues. The main challenges are:

1. Extracting emotional clues.

Most methods (Hazarika et al., 2018a, b; Jiao et al., 2020b) retrieve relevant context from static memory, limiting their ability to capture richer emotional clues.

2. Integrating emotional clues.

Many studies (Majumder et al., 2019; Ghosal et al., 2019; Lu et al., 2020) use attention mechanisms to integrate encoded emotional clues, but ignore their intrinsic semantic order. This loses logical relationships between clues and makes it difficult to capture the key factors that trigger emotion.

Cognitive Theory of Emotion (Schachter and Singer, 1962; Scherer et al., 2001) argues that cognitive factors play a potentially decisive role in the formation of emotional states. These cognitive factors can be captured by repeatedly executing intuitive retrieval and conscious reasoning processes in the brain (Evans, 1984, 2003, 2008; Sloman, 1996). Inspired by this idea, the paper tries to model these two key processes to reason over emotional clues and fully understand conversational context. By following the mechanism of working memory (Baddeley, 1992) in the cognition stage, the model can repeatedly perform these two cognitive processes to guide extraction and integration of emotional clues, simulating a distinctively human cognitive process.

The paper proposes DialogueCRN to identify emotions in utterances by fully understanding dialogue context. The model introduces a cognition stage to extract and integrate emotional clues from context retrieved by the perception stage. First, in the perception stage, long short-term memory (LSTM) networks (Hochreiter and Schmidhuber, 1997) capture situational-level and speaker-level context. Based on these contexts, global memory is obtained to store different contextual information. Second, in the cognition stage, a multi-turn reasoning module repeatedly extracts and integrates emotional clues. This module performs two processes: intuitive retrieval and conscious reasoning. The former uses attention to retrieve and match contextual clues from global memory, imitating intuitive retrieval. The latter uses LSTM to learn intrinsic logical order and integrate contextual clues by maintaining and updating dynamic working memory. This imitates human reasoning: slower, but with distinct reasoning ability (Baddeley, 1992). Finally, an emotion classifier predicts utterance emotion labels based on contextual clues at the situational and speaker levels.

The model is evaluated on three public benchmark datasets: IEMOCAP, SEMAINE, and MELD. Experimental results consistently show that the proposed model significantly outperforms compared methods. Understanding emotional clues from a cognitive perspective improves emotion-recognition performance.

The main contributions are:

- The paper proposes DialogueCRN to fully understand dialogue context from a cognitive perspective. To the authors' knowledge, this is the first attempt to explore cognitive factors for emotion recognition in conversations.

- It designs a multi-turn reasoning module to extract and integrate emotional clues by iteratively performing intuitive retrieval and conscious reasoning, imitating human cognitive thinking.

- It conducts extensive experiments on three public benchmark datasets, and the results consistently demonstrate the effectiveness and superiority of the proposed model.

## 2 Method

### 2.1 Problem Statement

Formally, let \(U=[u_{1} ,u_{2},…,u_{N}]\) be a dialogue, where \(N\) is the number of utterances, and there are \(M\) speakers or parties \(p_{1},p_{2},…,p_{M}(M\ge2)\).

Each utterance \(u_{i}\) is spoken by speaker \(p_{\phi(u_{i})}\), where \(\phi\) maps an utterance index to the corresponding speaker index.

For each \(\lambda \in [1,M]\), define \(U_{\lambda}\) as the set of utterances spoken by speaker \(p_{\lambda}\):

\[U_{\lambda}=\{u_{i}|u_{i}\in U \text{ and } u_{i} \text{ is spoken by } p_{\lambda},\forall i\in[1,N]\}
\]

The ERC task is to predict the emotion label \(y_{i}\) for each utterance \(u_{i}\) from a predefined label set \(\Gamma\).

### 2.2 Text Features

Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) (Kim, 2014) can capture n-gram information from utterances. Following previous work (Hazarika et al., 2018b; Majumder et al., 2019; Ghosal et al., 2019), the model uses a CNN layer with max-pooling to extract context-independent text features from each utterance transcript. The input is a 300-dimensional pretrained GloVe vector trained on 840B tokens (Pennington et al., 2014). Three filters of sizes 3, 4, and 5 are used, each with 50 feature maps. These maps are processed with max-pooling and ReLU activation (Nair and Hinton, 2010). The activated features are concatenated and projected into a dense layer of dimension \(d_{u}=100\). The output forms the utterance representation. Let \(\{u_{i}\}_{i=1}^{N},u_{i}\in\mathbb{R} ^{d_{u}}\) denote the representations of the \(N\) utterances.

### 2.3 Model

DialogueCRN consists of three parts: the perception phase, the cognition phase, and the emotion classifier. The overall architecture is shown in Figure 1.

#### 2.3.1 Perception Phase

In the perception phase, based on input text features, the model first generates situational-level and speaker-level dialogue-context representations. Then it obtains global memory to store different contextual information.

**Dialogue Context Representation**

Long short-term memory (LSTM) introduces gate mechanisms into recurrent neural networks to capture long-term dependencies from input sequences. Two bidirectional LSTMs are used to capture context dependencies at the situational and speaker levels.

For situational-level context, a bidirectional LSTM captures sequential dependencies between adjacent utterances in the same dialogue. The input is the text feature \(u_{i}\in\mathbb{R} ^{d_{u}}\) for each utterance. The situational-level context representation \(c_{i}^{s}\in\mathbb{R} ^{2d_{u}}\) is computed as:

\[c_{i}^{s},h_{i}^{s}=\overleftrightarrow{LSTM}^{s}(u_{i},h_{i-1}^{s}),\tag{1}
\]

where \(h_{i-1}^{s}\in\mathbb{R} ^{d_{u}}\) is the hidden state of the situational-level LSTM at layer \(i\).

For speaker-level context, another bidirectional LSTM captures self-dependency between adjacent utterances from the same speaker. Given text feature \(u_{i}\), speaker-level context representation \(c_{i}^{v}\in\mathbb{R} ^{2d_{u}}\) is computed as:

\[c_{i}^{v},h_{\lambda,j}^{v}=\overleftrightarrow{LSTM}^{v}(u_{i},h_{\lambda,j-1}^{v}),j\in[1,|U_{\lambda}|],\tag{2}
\]

where \(\lambda=\phi(u_{i})\). \(U_{\lambda}\) denotes all utterances spoken by speaker \(p_{\lambda}\). \(h_{\lambda,j}^{v}\in\mathbb{R} ^{d_{u}}\) is the hidden state of the speaker-level LSTM for speaker \(p_{\lambda}\) at step \(j\).

**Global Memory Representation**

Based on the dialogue-context representations above, global memory is obtained through linear layers to store different contextual information. Situational-level context memory \(G^{s}=[g_{1}^{s},g_{2}^{s},…,g_{N}^{s}]\) and speaker-level context memory \(G^{v}=[g_{1}^{v},g_{2}^{v},…,g_{N}^{v}]\) are computed as:

\[g_{i}^{s}=W_{g}^{s}c_{i}^{s}+b_{g}^{s},\tag{3}
\]

\[g_{i}^{v}=W_{g}^{v}c_{i}^{v}+b_{g}^{v},\tag{4}
\]

where \(W_{g}^{s},W_{g}^{v}\in\mathbb{R} ^{2d_{u}\times 2d_{u}}\) and \(b_{g}^{s}, b_{g}^{v}\in\mathbb{R} ^{2d_{u}}\) are learnable parameters.

#### 2.3.2 Cognition Phase

Inspired by Cognitive Theory of Emotion, cognitive factors are considered potentially decisive in the formation of emotional states. Therefore, in the cognition phase, the authors design a multi-turn reasoning module to iteratively extract and integrate emotional clues. The reasoning module architecture is shown in Figure 2.

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15271830/image-02.png)

The reasoning module performs two processes: intuitive retrieval and conscious reasoning. At round \(t\), the reasoning process uses an LSTM to learn intrinsic logical order and integrate contextual clues into working memory. This is the slower process, but it has distinctive human reasoning power:

\[\tilde{q} _{i}^{(t-1)},h_{i}^{(t)}=\overrightarrow{LSTM}(q_{i}^{(t-1)},h_{i}^{(t-1)}),\tag{5}
\]

where \(\tilde{q} _{i}^{(t-1)}\in\mathbb{R} ^{2d_{u}}\) is the output vector. \(q _{i}^{(t)}\in\mathbb{R} ^{4d_{u}}\) is initialized by the current utterance context representation \(c_{i}\), i.e. \(q_{i}^{(0)}=W_{q}c_{i}+b_{q}\), where \(W_{q}\in\mathbb{R} ^{4d_{u}\times2d_{u}}\) and \(b_{q}\in\mathbb{R}^{4d_{u}}\) are learnable parameters. \(h_{i}^{(t)}\in\mathbb{R}^{2d_{u}}\) is the working memory. It stores and updates previous memory \(h_{i}^{(t-1)}\), and also guides extraction of clues in the next round. Through the sequential flow of working memory, the model learns implicit logical order among clues, similar to conscious human thinking. \(h_{i}^{(t)}\) is initialized as 0. \(t\) indicates how many processing steps are executed to compute the final state.

For retrieval, an attention mechanism matches relevant contextual clues from global memory:

\[\mathbf{e}_{i j}^{(t-1)}=f\left(\mathbf{g}_{j}, \tilde{\mathbf{q}}_{i}^{(t-1)}\right)\tag{6}
\]

\[\alpha_{i j}^{(t-1)}=\frac{\exp \left(\mathbf{e}_{i j}^{(t-1)}\right)}{\sum_{j=1}^{N} \exp \left(\mathbf{e}_{i j}^{(t-1)}\right)}\tag{7}
\]

\[\mathbf{r}_{i}^{(t-1)}=\sum_{j=1}^{N} \alpha_{i j}^{(t-1)} \mathbf{g}_{j}\tag{8}
\]

Here, \(f\) computes a scalar score from \(g_{i}\) to \(\tilde{\mathbf{q}}_{i}^{(t-1)}\), for example with a dot product.

Then the output of the reasoning process \(\tilde{\mathbf{q}}_{i}^{(t-1)}\) and the attention readout \(\mathbf{r}_{i}^{(t-1)}\) are concatenated to form the query for the next round:

\[\mathbf{q}_{i}^{(t)}=\left[\tilde{\mathbf{q}}_{i}^{(t-1)} ; \mathbf{r}_{i}^{(t-1)}\right]\tag{9}
\]

The query \(\mathbf{q}_{i}^{(t)}\) is updated under guidance from working memory \(h_{i}^{(t)}\), so it can retrieve more contextual clues from global memory.

In summary, given context \(c_{i}\), global memory \(G\), and number of rounds \(T\), the cognition phase can be represented as \(q_{i}=Cognition(c_{i},G;T)\). The paper designs two separate cognition phases to explore situational-level and speaker-level contextual clues:

\[\mathbf{q}_{i}^{s}=Cognition^{s}\left(\mathbf{c}_{i}^{s}, \mathbf{G}^{s} ; T^{s}\right),\tag{10}
\]

\[\mathbf{q}_{i}^{v}=Cognition^{v}\left(\mathbf{c}_{i}^{v}, \mathbf{G}^{v} ; T^{v}\right),\tag{11}
\]

where \(T^{s}\) and \(T^{v}\) are the numbers of cognition rounds for the situational-level and speaker-level cognition stages.

Based on these output vectors, the final representation \(\mathbf{o}\) is the concatenation of the two vectors:

\[\mathbf{o}_{i}=\left[\mathbf{q}_{i}^{s} ; \mathbf{q}_{i}^{v}\right],\tag{12}
\]

#### 2.3.3 Emotion Classifier

Finally, based on the contextual clues above, an emotion classifier predicts the emotion label of the utterance:

\[\hat{\mathbf{y}}_{i}=\operatorname{softmax}\left(\mathbf{W}_{o} \mathbf{o}_{i}+\mathbf{b}_{o}\right),\tag{13}
\]

where \(\mathbf{W}_{o} \in \mathbb{R}^{8 d_{u} \times|\mathcal{Y}|}\) and \(\mathbf{b}_{o} \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{Y}|}\) are trainable parameters. \(|\mathcal{Y}|\) is the number of emotion labels.

The model is trained with cross-entropy loss:

\[\mathcal{L}=-\frac{1}{\sum_{l=1}^{L} \tau(l)} \sum_{i=1}^{L} \sum_{k=1}^{\tau(i)} \mathbf{y}_{i, k}^{l} \log \left(\hat{\mathbf{y}}_{i, k}^{l}\right),\tag{14}
\]

where \(L\) is the total number of dialogues or examples in the training set, and \(\tau(i)\) is the number of utterances in example \(i\). \(\mathbf{y}_{i, k}^{l}\) and \(\hat{\mathbf{y}}_{i, k}^{l}\) are the one-hot vector and probability vector for emotion class \(k\) of utterance \(i\) in example \(l\).

## 3 Experimental Procedure

### 3.1 Datasets

The model is evaluated on three benchmark datasets: IEMOCAP (Busso et al., 2008), SEMAINE (McKeown et al., 2012), and MELD (Poria et al., 2019). Dataset statistics are shown in Table 1. These datasets are multimodal and contain text, visual, and acoustic features. This paper mainly studies emotion recognition in text conversations. Multimodal emotion recognition in conversations is left for future work.

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15271830/image-03.png)

**IEMOCAP**

IEMOCAP contains dyadic conversation videos from ten people. Only the first eight speakers from sessions 1 to 4 belong to the training group. Utterances are annotated with one of six emotion labels: happy, sad, neutral, angry, excited, and frustrated. Since IEMOCAP does not provide predefined training and validation splits, the validation set is extracted from the randomly shuffled training set at an 80:20 ratio, following previous studies.

**SEMAINE**

SEMAINE is a video database of human-agent interaction. It was used in the AVEC 2012 challenge, which required prediction of four continuous affective attributes: arousal, expectancy, power, and valence. In each video, gold annotations are available every 0.2 seconds. Following previous work, the attributes are averaged over the span of each utterance to obtain utterance-level annotations. The standard training and test splits from the challenge are used.

**MELD**

The Multimodal EmotionLines Dataset (MELD) is an extension of EmotionLines. It is collected from the TV series Friends and contains more than 1,400 multi-party dialogues and more than 13,000 utterances. Each utterance has one of seven emotion labels: happy/joy, anger, fear, disgust, sadness, surprise, and neutral. The predefined training and validation splits in MELD are used.

### 3.2 Compared Methods

The proposed model is compared with the following baselines.

**TextCNN**

A convolutional neural network trained on context-independent utterances.

**Memnet**

An end-to-end memory network that updates memory in multiple hops.

**bc-LSTM + Att**

Uses a bidirectional LSTM to capture contextual content from surrounding utterances. An attention mechanism reweights features and produces a more informative output.

**CMN**

Uses two different GRUs for two speakers to encode dialogue context from dialogue history.

**ICON**

Extends CMN by connecting outputs from individual speaker GRUs with another GRU to perceive inter-speaker modeling.

**DialogueRNN**

A recurrent network with two GRUs that tracks speaker state and context during a conversation.

**DialogueGCN**

A graph-based model where nodes represent utterances and edges represent dependencies between utterance speakers.

### 3.3 Evaluation Metrics

Following previous work, for IEMOCAP and MELD the paper uses accuracy (Acc.) to measure overall performance. It also reports weighted average F1 (Weighted-F1) and macro average F1 (Macro-F1) to evaluate performance on majority and minority classes respectively. For SEMAINE, it reports mean absolute error (MAE) for each attribute. Lower MAE means better detection performance.

### 3.4 Implementation Details

The validation set is used to tune hyperparameters. In the perception stage, the model uses two-layer bidirectional LSTMs on IEMOCAP and SEMAINE and a one-layer bidirectional LSTM on MELD. In the cognition stage, a one-layer LSTM is used on all datasets. Batch size is set to 32. Adam is used as the optimizer on IEMOCAP, SEMAINE, and MELD, with initial learning rates of {0.0001, 0.001, 0.001} and L2 decay of {0.0002, 0.0005, 0.0005}. Dropout is set to 0.2. All models are trained for at most 100 epochs, and training stops if validation loss does not decrease for 20 consecutive epochs.

For DialogueGCN and DialogueRNN, the authors run the public code provided by the original authors in the same environment.

## 4 Results and Analysis

### 4.1 Experimental Results

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15271830/image-04.png)

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15271830/image-05.png)

Tables 2, 3, and 4 show comparison results for emotion recognition in text conversations. DialogueCRN consistently performs better than compared models on all datasets, and the improvements are statistically significant under a paired t-test with \(p<0.05\).

**IEMOCAP and SEMAINE**

Both IEMOCAP and SEMAINE contain long conversations, with average length no less than 50. This indicates that both datasets contain rich contextual information. TextCNN ignores dialogue context and obtains the worst performance. Memnet and bc-LSTM + Att perceive situational-level context for the current utterance. CMN perceives speaker-level context.

Memnet, bc-LSTM + Att, and CMN slightly outperform TextCNN. ICON, DialogueRNN, and DialogueGCN consider both situational-level and speaker-level context to simulate the perception stage of context, and perform better than the previous models. Compared with baselines, DialogueCRN can extract and integrate emotional clues by exploring cognitive factors. Therefore, it obtains better evaluation scores. On IEMOCAP, DialogueCRN improves over the previous best baseline by 3.2%, 4.0%, and 4.7% in Acc., Weighted-F1, and Macro-F1 respectively. On SEMAINE, DialogueCRN obtains an 11.1% improvement in MAE for the arousal attribute.

**MELD**

According to Table 1, MELD has many speakers per dialogue, up to 9, and an average dialogue length of 10. Shorter dialogue length means it contains less contextual information. Interestingly, Table 4 shows that TextCNN, which ignores dialogue context, performs better than most baselines. This suggests that when context is limited or missing, perceiving it may not learn useful features. DialogueGCN uses graph structure to perceive interactions among multiple speakers, which is enough to perceive speaker-level context, so performance improves slightly. Compared with other baselines, DialogueCRN performs sequential thinking over context and understands emotional clues from a cognitive perspective. It obtains the best results, such as a 2.9% improvement in Weighted-F1.

### 4.2 Ablation Study

To better understand the contribution of different modules in DialogueCRN, the paper conducts several ablation experiments on IEMOCAP and SEMAINE. Different modules that simulate situational-level and speaker-level context are removed from the perception and cognition stages respectively. Results are shown in Table 5. When cognition and perception modules are removed one after another, performance drops significantly, indicating that both cognition and perception stages are important for ERC.

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15271830/image-06.png)

**Effect of the Cognition Stage**

When only the cognition stage is removed, Acc., Weighted-F1, and Macro-F1 on IEMOCAP drop by 4.3%, 4.3%, and 6.5% respectively. On SEMAINE, MAE for valence, arousal, and expectancy increases by 2.3%, 12.5%, and 2.9%. These results show the effectiveness of the cognition stage, which can consciously and sequentially reason over contextual information obtained by perception. When cognition over either speaker-level or situational-level context is removed, results decline on all datasets, showing that both situational and speaker factors are crucial in the cognition stage.

**Effect of the Perception Stage**

When the perception module is removed, performance drops sharply. This lower result shows the necessity of the perception stage, which unconsciously matches relevant context based on the current utterance.

**Effect of Different Contexts**

When situational-level or speaker-level context is removed separately from the cognition or perception stage, performance declines. This indicates that both types of context are useful in both perception and cognition stages. The drop differs between datasets: speaker-level context plays a larger role in the perception stage, while the more complex situational-level context works well in the cognition stage. Intuitive matching in perception has limited ability to learn informative features from context, while conscious cognitive reasoning promotes better understanding.

### 4.3 Parameter Analysis

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15271830/image-07.png)

The paper studies performance with respect to the number of rounds in the cognition stage. Figure 3 shows that the best \(\{T^{s}，T^{v}\}\) on IEMOCAP and SEMAINE are {2, 2} and {1, 3}, with Weighted-F1 of 66.20% on IEMOCAP and MAE of 0.1522 for the arousal attribute on SEMAINE. It is worth noting that SEMAINE needs more rounds in the speaker-level cognition stage. This suggests that speaker-level contextual clues may be more important for arousal emotion, especially empathetic clues that require complex reasoning.

When only situational-level or speaker-level context is considered in the cognition stage, results on both datasets improve significantly within a certain number of rounds. This shows that the multi-turn reasoning module is effective for understanding contextual clues.

### 4.4 Case Study

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15271830/image-08.png)

Figure 4 shows a dialogue sampled from IEMOCAP. The goal is to predict the emotion label of utterance 8. Methods such as DialogueRNN and DialogueGCN lack the ability to consciously understand emotional clues, such as the cause of emotion, in this case failed expectation. They can easily misclassify the emotion as anger or neutral.

DialogueCRN can understand dialogue context through perception and cognition. In the cognition stage, two processes are repeatedly performed: the intuitive retrieval process 8-7-2-1, shown by blue arrows, and the conscious reasoning process a-b-c, shown by red arrows. These processes extract and integrate emotional clues. The conclusion is that utterance 8 implies that the woman did not receive the extra compensation she expected. ## 5 Related Work

### 5.1 Emotion Recognition

Emotion recognition (ER) has received increasing attention in natural language processing (NLP) and artificial intelligence (AI). Existing studies usually formulate ER as a classification task over context-free data blocks, such as individual reviews or documents. They can roughly be divided into feature-engineering methods (Devillers and Vidrascu, 2006) and deep-learning methods (Tang et al., 2016; Wei et al., 2020).

### 5.2 Emotion Recognition in Conversations

In recent years, ERC has attracted attention from researchers. Unlike traditional emotion recognition, situational-level and speaker-level context play important roles in identifying the emotion of utterances in dialogue (Li et al., 2020). Ignoring them leads to limited performance (Bertero et al., 2016). Existing studies usually use deep learning methods to obtain contextual features for ERC and can be divided into sequence-based and graph-based methods.

**Sequence-Based Methods**

Many studies capture contextual information from utterance sequences. Poria et al. (2017) use LSTM to capture dialogue contextual features. Hazarika et al. (2018a, b) use end-to-end memory networks to capture contextual features while distinguishing speakers. Zhong et al. (2019) and Li et al. (2020) use Transformers to capture richer contextual features with attention. Majumder et al. (2019) introduce speaker states and global states for each dialogue using GRUs. Jiao et al. (2020a) introduce a dialogue completion task to learn from unsupervised dialogue data. Jiao et al. (2020b) propose a hierarchical memory network for real-time emotion recognition without future context. Wang et al. (2020) model ERC as sequence tagging to learn emotions consistently and sequentially. Lu et al. (2020) propose an iterative emotion interaction network to explicitly model emotional interaction.

**Graph-Based Methods**

Some studies model dialogue environments by designing specific graph structures (Zhang et al., 2019; Ghosal et al., 2019; Ishiwatari et al., 2020; Lian et al., 2020). They use graph neural networks (Kipf and Welling, 2017; Velickovic et al., 2017) to capture multiple dependencies in dialogue and have achieved good performance.

Different from previous studies, this paper is inspired by cognitive theory of emotion and is the first attempt to explore cognitive factors for emotion recognition in conversations. To fully understand dialogue context, the authors propose DialogueCRN to extract and integrate rich emotional clues in a cognitive way.

## 6 Conclusion

This paper studies cognitive factors in emotion recognition in conversations. It proposes DialogueCRN to fully understand situational-level and speaker-level context. DialogueCRN introduces a cognition stage to extract and integrate emotional clues from context retrieved in the perception stage. In the cognition stage, a multi-turn reasoning module iteratively performs intuitive retrieval and conscious reasoning, imitating human cognitive thinking. The model obtains emotional clues that trigger the current emotion and uses them for better classification. Experiments on three benchmark datasets demonstrate the effectiveness and superiority of the model. The case study shows that considering cognitive factors can better understand emotional clues and improve ERC performance.
