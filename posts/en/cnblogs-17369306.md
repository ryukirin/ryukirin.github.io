# Revisiting Linear Regression

## Lecture Translation

### Linear Regression

- One of the most classical statistical models, but still widely used.

- Models the input-output relationship with a linear function.

\[y_{i}=\beta_1 x_{i 1}+\beta_2 x_{i 2}+\cdots+\beta_p x_{i p}+\varepsilon_i
\]

where:

\(y_{i}\) is the output, or prediction target.

- \(\boldsymbol{X}_i=\left(x_{i 1}, \ldots, x_{i p}\right)^{\top}\) is the input vector.

- \(\boldsymbol{\beta}=\left(\beta_1, \ldots, \beta_p\right)^{\top}\) is the unknown parameter vector.

- \(\varepsilon_i\) is observation noise, assumed to satisfy \(\mathbb{E}\left[\epsilon_i\right]=0\), \(\mathbb{V}\left[\epsilon_i\right]=\sigma^2\), and \(\left\{\epsilon_i\right\}_{i=1}^n\) are mutually independent. See the appendix for details.

Estimate \(\beta_i\) from a dataset containing \(\left(x_i, y_i\right)\).

### Examples

- `y`: sales volume, `x`: product information.

- `y`: fuel efficiency, `x`: engine design.

- `y`: electricity consumption, `x`: consumption history and temperature.

- `y`: toxicity, `x`: chemical composition information.

### Vector and Matrix Notation

- Training dataset \(\left\{\left(x_{i},y_{i}\right)\right\}_{i=1}^{n}\)

\[\boldsymbol{X}=\left[\begin{array}{c}
\boldsymbol{x}_{1}^{\top} \\
\vdots \\
\boldsymbol{x}_{n}^{\top}
\end{array}\right]=\left[\begin{array}{ccc}
x_{11} & \cdots & x_{1 p} \\
\vdots & & \vdots \\
x_{n 1} & \cdots & x_{n p}
\end{array}\right] \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times p}, \boldsymbol{y}=\left[\begin{array}{c}
y_{1} \\
\vdots \\
y_{n}
\end{array}\right] \in \mathbb{R}^{n}
\]

- The model can also be written as

\[\boldsymbol{y}=\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}+\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}
\]

where \(\boldsymbol{\beta}=\left[\beta_{1}, \ldots, \beta_{p}\right]^{\top}\), \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}=\left[\varepsilon_{1}, \ldots, \varepsilon_{n}\right]^{\top}\).

Note that \(\boldsymbol{y}\) is a random variable because \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\) is random.

- \(\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon]=\mathbf{0}\), \(\mathbb{V}[\varepsilon]=\sigma^{2} \boldsymbol{I}\), where \(\boldsymbol{I}\) is the identity matrix.

### Least-Squares Estimator (LSE)

- Sum of squared errors

\[\begin{aligned}
\sum_{n}^{i=1} (y_i-x_i^{\top}\beta)^2 & = \begin{bmatrix}y_1-x_1^{\top}\beta&\cdots&y_n-x_n^{\top}\beta\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}
 y_1-x_1^{\top}\beta\\\vdots
 \\y_n-x_n^{\top}\beta\end{bmatrix}\\
& = (\boldsymbol{y}-\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\beta})^{\top}(\boldsymbol{y}-\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\beta})\\
& = \begin{Vmatrix}
\boldsymbol{y}-\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}
\end{Vmatrix}^{2}
\end{aligned}
\]

- Least-squares estimator

\[\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} =\underset{\beta }{argmin} \begin{Vmatrix}
\boldsymbol{y}-\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}
\end{Vmatrix}^{2}
\]

This is a convex quadratic minimization problem and can be solved analytically.

### Closed-Form Solution of LSE

- Take the derivative of the sum of squared errors:

\[\frac{\partial}{\partial \boldsymbol{\beta}}\|\boldsymbol{y}-\boldsymbol{X} \boldsymbol{\beta}\|^{2}
\]

- Because \(\|\boldsymbol{y}-\boldsymbol{x} \boldsymbol{\beta}\|^{2}\) is convex, the minimum can be determined by setting the derivative to 0.

\[\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial}{\partial \boldsymbol{\beta}}\|\boldsymbol{y}-\boldsymbol{X} \boldsymbol{\beta}\|^{2}
& = \frac{\partial}{\partial \boldsymbol{\beta}}(\boldsymbol{y}-\boldsymbol{X} \boldsymbol{\beta})^{\top}(\boldsymbol{y}-\boldsymbol{X} \boldsymbol{\beta})\\
& = \frac{\partial}{\partial \boldsymbol{\beta}}(\boldsymbol{y}^{\top }\boldsymbol{y}-\boldsymbol{y}^{\top }\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}-(\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\beta})^{\top}\boldsymbol{y}-(\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\beta})^{\top}(\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}))
\end{aligned}
\]

where

\[\begin{aligned}
(\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\beta})^{\top}\boldsymbol{y}
& = \boldsymbol{\beta}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{y}\\
\boldsymbol{y}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}
& = \boldsymbol{\beta}^{\top}(\boldsymbol{y}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{\top}\\
& = \boldsymbol{\beta}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{y}
\end{aligned}
\]

Therefore,

\[\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial}{\partial \boldsymbol{\beta}}\|\boldsymbol{y}-\boldsymbol{X} \boldsymbol{\beta}\|^{2}
& = \frac{\partial}{\partial \boldsymbol{\beta}}(\boldsymbol{y}-\boldsymbol{X} \boldsymbol{\beta})^{\top}(\boldsymbol{y}-\boldsymbol{X} \boldsymbol{\beta})\\
& = \frac{\partial}{\partial \boldsymbol{\beta}}(\boldsymbol{y}^{\top }\boldsymbol{y}-\boldsymbol{y}^{\top }\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}-(\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\beta})^{\top}\boldsymbol{y}-(\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\beta})^{\top}(\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}))\\
& = \frac{\partial}{\partial \boldsymbol{\beta}}(\boldsymbol{y}^{\top }\boldsymbol{y}-2\boldsymbol{\beta}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{y}-(\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\beta})^{\top}(\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}))\\
& = -2\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{y}+2\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}
\end{aligned}
\]

Set

\[\begin{aligned}
-2\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{y}+2\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}
& = 0
\end{aligned}
\]

\[\begin{aligned}
\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}
& = \boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{y}
\end{aligned}
\]

Assuming \((\boldsymbol{x}^{\top}\boldsymbol{x})^{-1}\) exists, the LSE is

\[\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}=(\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{y}
\]

This is the normal equation.

### Estimation Bias and Variance

- When \(\boldsymbol{y}\) is random, \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}=(\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{y}\) is also random.

An estimate computed from random data is itself random.

\(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}\) follows some distribution. From that distribution, we only observe one realization calculated from the given dataset.

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-17369306/image-02.png)

Because \(\mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{y}]=\mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{X} \boldsymbol{\beta}+\varepsilon]=\boldsymbol{X} \boldsymbol{\beta}\), we have

\[\begin{aligned}
\mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{\hat{\beta}}]
& = \mathbb{E}[(\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{y}]\\
& = (\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{y}]\\
& = (\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}
\end{aligned}
\]

Since

\[(\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X}=\boldsymbol{I}
\]

we get

\[\mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{\hat{\beta}}] = \boldsymbol{\beta}
\]

Because for a constant matrix \(\boldsymbol{M}\) and a random vector \(\boldsymbol{a}\),

\[\mathbb{V}[\boldsymbol{M} \boldsymbol{a}]=\boldsymbol{M} \mathbb{V}[\boldsymbol{a}] \boldsymbol{M}^{\top}
\]

and \(\mathbb{V}[\varepsilon]=\sigma^{2} \boldsymbol{I}\), we have

\[\begin{aligned}
\mathbb{V}[\boldsymbol{\hat{\beta}}]
& = \mathbb{V}[(\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{y}]\\
& = (\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\mathbb{V}[\boldsymbol{y}]\boldsymbol{X}(\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}\\
& = (\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\sigma^{2} \boldsymbol{I}\boldsymbol{X}(\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}\\
& = \sigma^{2}(\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X}(\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}\\
& = \sigma^{2}(\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}
\end{aligned}
\]

- These distributional properties have been widely studied in statistics because they help explain the strengths of LSE. They are also useful for hypothesis testing, although this course does not discuss that.

Here, we are considering \(\hat{\beta}\).

### Estimation Bias and Estimation Variance

The previous section already derived the result.

- \(\hat{\beta}\) is called an unbiased estimator.

- The equation for estimation variance helps evaluate the uncertainty of the estimator.

- Gauss-Markov theorem. See the appendix for details.

Among all unbiased linear estimators, LSE has the smallest estimation variance. Therefore, LSE is called the best linear unbiased estimator, or BLUE.

## Appendix

### Norm

For \(\boldsymbol{a}=\left(a_{1}, \ldots, a_{n}\right)^{\top}\), the norm is

\[\|\boldsymbol{a}\|=\sqrt{a_{1}^{2}+\cdots+a_{n}^{2}}
\]

### Vector Derivatives

In general, for a function `f` with respect to a vector \(\boldsymbol{v}=\left(v_{1}, \ldots, v_{n}\right)\),

\[\frac{\partial f(\boldsymbol{v})}{\partial \boldsymbol{v}}=\left[\begin{array}{c}
\frac{\partial f(\boldsymbol{v})}{\partial v_{1}} \\
\vdots \\
\frac{\partial f(\boldsymbol{v})}{\partial v_{n}}
\end{array}\right]
\]

For common vector derivatives, see section 2.4 of the [Matrix Cookbook](https://www.math.uwaterloo.ca/~hwolkowi/matrixcookbook.pdf).

### Expectation and Variance

- For a continuous random variable \(x\in \mathbb{R}\),

\[\mathbb{E}[x]=\int_{x} x p(x) \mathrm{d} x
\]

If `x` is a discrete variable \(x\in \mathcal{X}\), where \(\mathcal{X}\) is a set of candidate values,

\[\mathbb{E}[x]=\sum_{x \in \mathcal{X}} x p(x)
\]

- Variance

\[\mathbb{V}[x]=\mathbb{E}\left[(x-\mathbb{E}[x])^{2}\right]
\]

The covariance of two random variables `x` and `y` is

\[\operatorname{Cov}(x, y)=\mathbb{E}[(x-\mathbb{E}[x])(y-\mathbb{E}[y])]
\]

### Expectation and Covariance Matrix

- \(\mathbb{E}\) denotes the expected value of a random variable. For a random vector \(\boldsymbol{a}=\left(a_{1}, \ldots, a_{n}\right)^{\top}\),

\[\mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{a}]=\left[\begin{array}{c}
\mathbb{E}\left[a_{1}\right] \\
\vdots \\
\mathbb{E}\left[a_{n}\right]
\end{array}\right]
\]

- For a random vector \(\boldsymbol{a}=\left(a_{1}, \ldots, a_{n}\right)^{\top}\), the covariance \(\mathbb{V}[\mathbf{a}]\) is

\[\mathbb{V}[\mathbf{a}]=\mathbb{E}\left[(\mathbf{a}-\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{a}])(\boldsymbol{a}-\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{a}])^{\top}\right]
\]

Note that the \(i,j\) element of \(\mathbb{V}[\mathbf{a}]\) corresponds to the covariance between \(a_i\) and \(a_j\).

For example, \(\operatorname{Cov}\left(a_{i}, a_{j}\right)=\mathbb{E}\left[\left(a_{i}-\mathbb{E}\left[a_{i}\right]\right)\left(a_{j}-\mathbb{E}\left[a_{j}\right]\right)\right]\).

### Covariance Matrix of a Linear Transformation

When \(\boldsymbol{M}\) is a constant matrix and \(\boldsymbol{a}\) is a random vector,

\[\mathbb{V}[\boldsymbol{M} \boldsymbol{a}]=\boldsymbol{M} \mathbb{V}[\boldsymbol{a}] \boldsymbol{M}^{\top}
\]

This can be proven from the definition of \(\mathbb{V}\):

\[\begin{aligned}
\mathbb{V}[\mathbf{M a}]
& =\mathbb{E}\left[(\mathbf{M a}-\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{M a}])(\boldsymbol{M a}-\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{M a}])^{\top}\right] \\
& =\boldsymbol{M} \mathbb{E}\left[(\mathbf{a}-\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{a}])(\boldsymbol{a}-\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{a}])^{\top}\right] M^{\top} \\
& =\boldsymbol{M}[\mathbf{a}] M^{\top}
\end{aligned}
\]

### Matrix Inequalities

- Matrix inequality for \(\boldsymbol{M}\):

\[\boldsymbol{M}≥0
\]

This means \(\boldsymbol{M}\) is a [positive semidefinite matrix](https://www.zhihu.com/tardis/zm/art/44860862?source_id=1003).

\[\boldsymbol{x}^{\top}\boldsymbol{M}\boldsymbol{x}≥0
\]

for \(\forall\boldsymbol{x}\). Equivalently, all eigenvalues of \(\boldsymbol{M}\) are non-negative.

- Therefore, \(\boldsymbol{A}≥\boldsymbol{B}\) means \(\boldsymbol{A}-\boldsymbol{B}≥0\), which means \(\boldsymbol{A}-\boldsymbol{B}\) is positive semidefinite.

### Gauss-Markov Theorem

- Suppose a matrix \(\boldsymbol{C}\in \mathbb{R}^{p×n}\) satisfies

\[\boldsymbol{\beta} = \mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{C}\boldsymbol{y}]
\]

\(\boldsymbol{C}\boldsymbol{y}\) is called an unbiased linear estimator because it is an unbiased linear transformation of \(\boldsymbol{y}\).

- When \(\boldsymbol{C}=(\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\), \(\boldsymbol{C}\boldsymbol{y}\) is the LSE.

- When \(\boldsymbol{\beta}=\mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{C}(\boldsymbol{X} \boldsymbol{\beta}+\varepsilon)] \Rightarrow \boldsymbol{\beta}=\boldsymbol{C} \boldsymbol{X} \boldsymbol{\beta} \Rightarrow \mathbf{I}=\boldsymbol{C} \boldsymbol{X}\), any \(\boldsymbol{C}\) satisfying \(\mathbf{I}=\boldsymbol{C} \boldsymbol{X}\) becomes unbiased, and such matrices can exist.

The Gauss-Markov theorem guarantees, omitting the [proof](https://zhuanlan.zhihu.com/p/34119477), that

\[\mathbb{V}[\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}] \leq \mathbb{V}[\boldsymbol{C y}]
\]

## Exercises

### Exercise 0: Short Quiz

Under appropriate assumptions, the expected value of the least-squares estimator equals the true value. In general, an estimator with this property is called an [A] estimator. What is [A]?

- Unbiased estimator.

### Exercise 1

Prove \(\mathbb{V}[\boldsymbol{\hat{\beta}}] = \sigma^{2}(\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}\).

- This was already proven above.

\[\begin{aligned}
\mathbb{V}[\boldsymbol{\hat{\beta}}]
& = \mathbb{V}[(\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{y}]\\
& = (\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\mathbb{V}[\boldsymbol{y}]\boldsymbol{X}(\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}\\
& = (\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\sigma^{2} \boldsymbol{I}\boldsymbol{X}(\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}\\
& = \sigma^{2}(\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X}(\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}\\
& = \sigma^{2}(\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}
\end{aligned}
\]

### Exercise 2

Let

\[\mathbf{X}=\left[\begin{array}{cc}
x_{11} & x_{12} \\
\vdots & \vdots \\
x_{n 2} & x_{n 2}
\end{array}\right]
\]

Assume \(\mathbf{X}\) satisfies:

- The mean of each dimension is zero:

\(\bar{x}_{j}=\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_{i j}=0(\text { for } j=1,2)\)

- The variance of each dimension is one:

\(\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(x_{i j}-\bar{x}_{j}\right)^{2}=\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_{i j}^{2}=1(\text { for } j=1,2)\)

- The covariance of the two dimensions is zero:

\(\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n}\left(x_{i 1}-\bar{x}_{1}\right)\left(x_{i 2}-\bar{x}_{2}\right)=\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_{i 1} x_{i 2}=0\)

Write out \(\mathbb{V}[\boldsymbol{\hat{\beta}}]\).

- \[\begin{aligned}
\mathbb{V}[\boldsymbol{\hat{\beta}}]
& = \sigma^{2}(\boldsymbol{X}^{\top}\boldsymbol{X})^{-1}\\
& = \sigma^{2}(\left[\begin{array}{cc}
x_{11} & \cdots & x_{n1} \\
x_{12} & \cdots & x_{n 2}
\end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{cc}
x_{11} & x_{12} \\
\vdots & \vdots \\
x_{n 2} & x_{n 2}
\end{array}\right])^{-1}\\
& = \sigma^{2}\left[\begin{array}{cc}
\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_{i2}^2 & \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_{i1}x_{i2}\\
\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_{i1}x_{i2} & \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_{i2}^2
\end{array}\right]^{-1}\\
& = \sigma^{2}\left[\begin{array}{cc}
n & 0\\
0 & n
\end{array}\right]^{-1}\\
& = \sigma^{2}\left[\begin{array}{cc}
\frac{1}{n}  & 0\\
0 & \frac{1}{n}
\end{array}\right]\\
\end{aligned}
\]
