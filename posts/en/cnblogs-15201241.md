### Local extrema and absolute extrema

Local extrema are about a local neighborhood, while absolute extrema are about the whole domain.

A discontinuity can be an extremum point.

### Criteria for monotonicity and extrema

#### Criteria for monotonicity

If the derivative of `f(x)` is `<0` on an interval, then the function is strictly decreasing on that interval.

If the derivative of `f(x)` is `>0` on an interval, then the function is strictly increasing on that interval.

#### Necessary condition for a first-order differentiable point to be an extremum

Suppose `f(x)` is differentiable at `x=x0`, and it reaches an extremum at `x0`. Then necessarily `f'(x0)=0`.

#### Inflection points

An inflection point is the boundary point between concave and convex arcs on a continuous curve.

### Criteria for concavity, convexity, and inflection points

#### Concavity and convexity

Suppose `f(x)` is twice differentiable on an interval.

If the second derivative is `>0` on the interval, the graph is concave, or downward-convex, on that interval.

If the second derivative is `<0` on the interval, the graph is convex, or upward-convex, on that interval.

#### Necessary condition for a twice-differentiable point to be an inflection point

Suppose `f''(x)` exists, and `(x0,f(x0))` is an inflection point on the curve. Then `f''(x0)=0`.

#### Sufficient conditions for identifying inflection points

- First sufficient condition: in a punctured neighborhood, the second derivative has opposite signs on the two sides of the point

![](/assets/posts/cnblogs-15201241/image-08.png)

- Second sufficient condition: third derivative

Suppose `f(x)` is three-times differentiable in a neighborhood of `x=x0`, with `f''(x0)=0` and `f'''(x0)≠0`. Then `(x0,f(x0))` is an inflection point.
